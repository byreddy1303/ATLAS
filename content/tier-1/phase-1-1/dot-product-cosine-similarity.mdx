export const highDimCode = `import numpy as np

rng = np.random.default_rng(42)
# Sample 5000 pairs of random unit vectors in D dimensions,
# and record their cosine similarities.
for D in [2, 3, 10, 100, 1000]:
    v = rng.standard_normal((5000, D))
    v /= np.linalg.norm(v, axis=1, keepdims=True)   # unit-normalize

export const attentionMiniCode = `import numpy as np

# 4 "token" vectors in 3-D (imagine token embeddings)
tokens = np.array([
    [1.0, 0.0, 0.0],   # 0 : "the"
    [0.9, 0.1, 0.2],   # 1 : "big"
    [0.2, 0.9, 0.1],   # 2 : "dog"
    [0.1, 0.8, 0.2],   # 3 : "barks"
])

# query = token 2 ("dog"). We want to know how much each other token
# should "attend to" this query.
q = tokens[2]

# raw scores = dot products (this is exactly transformer attention,
# minus the /sqrt(d_k) scaling and softmax)
scores = tokens @ q                    # (4,)
print("raw scores :", scores.round(3))

# softmax to turn scores into weights
expv    = np.exp(scores - scores.max())
weights = expv / expv.sum()
print("weights    :", weights.round(3))

# the attended output is a weighted sum of the tokens
out = weights @ tokens
print("output     :", out.round(3))`;

export const solutionCode = `import numpy as np

def most_similar_cities(home, cities, k=5):
    # cities: (n, d) matrix, home: (d,)
    dots = cities @ home
    home_norm = (home @ home) ** 0.5
    city_norms = (cities * cities).sum(axis=1) ** 0.5
    sims = dots / (city_norms * home_norm)
    return np.argsort(-sims)[:k]`;

<Motivation title="Why the dot product had to be invented">

A user just finished *Arrival*. Out of ten thousand movies, which are most similar to what they enjoyed? Every movie is five numbers — `[sci-fi, romance, action, philosophy, pace]` — and the user's taste is five numbers too. "Similar" has to become arithmetic.

Here's the observation that shapes everything. Taste = `(9, 3, 2, 9, 3)`. Movie B = `(90, 30, 20, 90, 30)` — the same *proportions*, ten times the magnitude. Movie C = `(3, 9, 9, 3, 9)` — smaller numbers, but the wrong *shape*. B is a great match and C is a bad one, even though C's raw numbers are "closer" in size. **What matters is direction, not magnitude.**

The **dot product** is the operation that measures alignment between two arrows; divide it by the two lengths and magnitude vanishes entirely, leaving **cosine similarity** — a pure measure of direction. This one identity powers every semantic search engine, every RAG pipeline, every recommender, and every attention head in every transformer. Get this chapter and you will recognize half of modern AI when you read its source code.

</Motivation>

<Intuition title="The idea, before any symbols">

Picture two arrows shot from the same point. If they point in nearly the same direction, they *agree*. If they point in perpendicular directions, they *disagree*. If they point in opposite directions, they *contradict*. The dot product is a single number that captures where on this "agree → perpendicular → contradict" scale two vectors sit. Positive means "kind of agree." Large positive means "strongly agree." Zero means "unrelated, right-angled." Negative means "actively opposite."

But the raw dot product doesn't know how big the arrows are. A short arrow and a long arrow pointing in exactly the same direction give a *big* dot product, not because they agree more strongly, but because one is long. To strip away that "size effect," you divide by the two lengths. What you're left with is a number between −1 and +1 — the **cosine of the angle** between the arrows. It's a pure measure of *how aligned they are*, ignoring how big either one is.

That's the whole chapter. Everything else — the algebraic formula, the projection interpretation, the code, the movie recommender, the transformer attention scores — is an implication of this single mental picture. Return to it whenever you get lost.

</Intuition>

<Recall term="Vector length (norm)">

For a 2-D vector `v = (v₁, v₂)`, the length is `‖v‖ = √(v₁² + v₂²)` — Pythagoras' theorem. For any dimension, `‖v‖ = √(v₁² + v₂² + ... + vₙ²) = √(v · v)`. Length is always non-negative, and zero only for the zero vector.

</Recall>

<Recall term="Angle between vectors">

Two arrows shot from the origin form an angle θ, measured as the smallest rotation from one to the other, so θ ∈ [0°, 180°]. Identical direction: θ = 0°. Perpendicular: θ = 90°. Opposite: θ = 180°. And `cos θ` is +1 at 0°, 0 at 90°, −1 at 180° — exactly the agree → perpendicular → contradict scale.

</Recall>

## The geometric definition — projection × length

Take vectors `a` and `b`, both drawn from the origin. Shine a lamp perpendicular to `b`: the **shadow** of `a` on the line containing `b` is the **scalar projection** of `a` onto `b` — how far along `b` does `a` reach when squashed onto `b`'s direction? Same direction: the shadow is the full length of `a`. Perpendicular: the shadow is zero. Opposite: the shadow is negative, falling on the wrong side of the origin.

The geometric definition of the dot product is the shadow times the length of `b`:

$$
\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \|\mathbf{a}\| \, \|\mathbf{b}\| \cos\theta
$$

Read it out loud: length of `a`, times length of `b`, times the cosine of the angle between them. Doubling either length doubles the dot product. Making the angle 90° zeros it. Making it 180° flips the sign.

Why multiply by `‖b‖` instead of reporting the shadow directly? Symmetry. The shadow of `a` on `b` is not the shadow of `b` on `a` — but `‖a‖ ‖b‖ cos θ` reads equally as "length of `a` × projection of `b` onto `a`" or "length of `b` × projection of `a` onto `b`", so `a · b = b · a`.

Physics got here first: work done by a constant force is `W = F · d` — the component of force *along the motion*, times the distance moved. Force perpendicular to motion does zero work. Every appearance of the dot product answers the same question: *how much of one vector points along another?*

**Mental picture to build:** two arrows, an angle, a light source. Every time you see `a · b`, imagine that scene.

## The algebraic definition — and why it agrees

The definition you likely met first in school looks nothing like the geometry:

$$
\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + \dots + a_n b_n = \sum_{i=1}^n a_i b_i
$$

Multiply matching components; sum them up. For `a = (2, 1)` and `b = (1, 2)`: `2·1 + 1·2 = 4`. No lengths, no angles — yet it produces the same number as the geometric formula. Here's the proof that glues them together. The triangle formed by `a`, `b`, and `a − b` obeys the *law of cosines*:

$$
\|\mathbf{a} - \mathbf{b}\|^2 = \|\mathbf{a}\|^2 + \|\mathbf{b}\|^2 - 2\|\mathbf{a}\|\|\mathbf{b}\|\cos\theta
$$

Expand the left side in coordinates: `‖a − b‖² = Σ(aᵢ − bᵢ)² = Σaᵢ² − 2Σaᵢbᵢ + Σbᵢ²`. Equate the two sides — the `Σaᵢ²` cancels with `‖a‖²`, the `Σbᵢ²` cancels with `‖b‖²`, leaving:

$$
-2\sum_i a_i b_i = -2 \|\mathbf{a}\|\|\mathbf{b}\|\cos\theta
$$

Divide by −2:

$$
\sum_i a_i b_i = \|\mathbf{a}\|\|\mathbf{b}\|\cos\theta
$$

The algebraic and geometric definitions *must* be equal. From now on you pick whichever description fits the moment: geometry to *reason*, arithmetic to *compute*.

## The code you'll actually write

No working engineer writes the sum by hand. In NumPy there are three interchangeable forms:

The generator-sum version is for teaching. The `@` operator is the modern form (added in Python 3.5 for exactly this purpose). `np.dot` is the historical spelling, still fine.

Speed is why the library form matters: real work computes *millions* of dot products per query — every query embedding against every stored document, every token against every other token. A Python-loop dot product over 1,000 dimensions costs microseconds; NumPy's C loop costs nanoseconds. At vector-database scale (100M+ embeddings), even NumPy is too slow, and systems like FAISS or pgvector build indices so "top-10 nearest by cosine" runs in log time — but the primitive in the innermost loop is still `a @ b`.

<Derivation title="Deriving cos θ = (a · b) / (‖a‖ ‖b‖)">

We already have two definitions of the dot product. Setting them equal solves for cos θ. Every line of the derivation gets a plain-English partner so you can read the math without any leaps.

<Step n={1} math={<span>Start with the geometric definition: <br/> <code>a · b = ‖a‖ · ‖b‖ · cos θ</code></span>}>
    This is the picture: dot product equals (length of a) × (length of b) × (cosine of the angle between them). Convince yourself with a sanity case. If θ = 90° (the vectors are perpendicular), then cos θ = 0, so a · b = 0. Perpendicular vectors have zero dot product. Now check the other extreme: if θ = 0° (the vectors point exactly the same way), cos θ = 1, so a · b = ‖a‖ · ‖b‖ — the product of the two lengths, its largest possible value. Both cases match the intuition; the formula is trustworthy.
</Step>

<Step n={2} math={<span>Divide both sides by <code>‖a‖ · ‖b‖</code>:<br/> <code>cos θ = (a · b) / (‖a‖ · ‖b‖)</code></span>}>
    Dividing by the two lengths strips away every trace of magnitude from both sides. What remains on the right — cosine — depends only on the *angle* between the vectors. This is the single move that turns "raw similarity" into "direction-only similarity". It is the mathematical reason cosine similarity works even when your movie vectors, document embeddings, or user-taste vectors all have wildly different scales. This is why a 20-page document and a 200-page document can still be compared honestly.
</Step>

<Step n={3} math={<span>Rewrite the numerator using the algebraic form:<br/> <code>cos θ = (a₁b₁ + a₂b₂ + ... + aₙbₙ) / (‖a‖ · ‖b‖)</code></span>}>
    Now every part of the formula is computable with plain arithmetic. No angles need to be measured, no protractors used, no trigonometry looked up. The numerator is a sum of products. The denominator is a product of two square-roots-of-sums-of-squares. If you can add and multiply, you can compute a cosine similarity in any number of dimensions. This is what makes cosine practical at Google-scale search: a plain arithmetic loop, nothing exotic.
</Step>

<Step n={4} math={<span>Establish the range: <code>−1 ≤ cos θ ≤ 1</code></span>}>
    Since θ lives in [0°, 180°] by convention, cosine goes through its full range. cos θ = 1 means the vectors point *identically* (angle 0°). cos θ = 0 means they're orthogonal (angle 90°). cos θ = −1 means they point in exactly opposite directions (angle 180°). Any embedding-search algorithm you'll ever write consumes this three-point scale. When you look at retrieval results, ranked by cosine similarity, you're always reading them along this dimension.
</Step>

<Step n={5} math={<span>Sanity: what if either vector is zero?</span>}>
    The denominator becomes zero and the expression is undefined. Geometrically this makes sense — you can't measure the angle between an arrow and "no arrow at all." Real systems that use cosine similarity either (a) filter zero-vectors before comparison, (b) clamp norms above some tiny ε, or (c) use raw dot product when they know the vectors are pre-normalized. The failure mode is annoying but always debuggable if you remember step 5.
</Step>

</Derivation>

<Example title="Working it by hand — a full trace">

Let `a = (2, 1)` and `b = (1, 2)`. We'll compute every quantity and see the picture form.

**Algebraic dot product.** `a · b = a₁b₁ + a₂b₂ = 2·1 + 1·2 = 2 + 2 = 4`. That's the scalar shadow-times-length quantity from the geometric definition.

**Lengths.** `‖a‖ = √(2² + 1²) = √5 ≈ 2.236`. Same for `b`: `‖b‖ = √(1² + 2²) = √5 ≈ 2.236`. The two vectors have identical lengths.

**Cosine similarity.** `cos θ = (a · b) / (‖a‖ · ‖b‖) = 4 / (√5 · √5) = 4 / 5 = 0.8`. Read this: "the two arrows are 80% of the way toward being identical" — closer to alignment than to perpendicularity.

**Angle.** `θ = arccos(0.8) ≈ 36.87°`. Confirm with a picture: the vector `(2, 1)` points 26.57° above the x-axis; the vector `(1, 2)` points 63.43° above the x-axis. The difference is 36.87°. ✓

**Now scale `b` by 100.** Let `b' = (100, 200)`. The dot product blows up: `a · b' = 2·100 + 1·200 = 400`. But the cosine similarity is unchanged: `cos θ = 400 / (√5 · √50000) = 400 / (√5 · 100√5) = 400 / 500 = 0.8`. The cosine formula is *invariant* to any positive scaling of either vector. That is the property that makes it useful for real recommenders where document lengths vary wildly.

Move the tips around in the playground below and watch how cos θ changes. Confirm: when the vectors are exactly aligned, cos θ = 1. When they're perpendicular, cos θ = 0. When one is anti-parallel to the other, cos θ = −1. When they're close in direction, cos θ ≈ 0.9-something. Build the reflex now so it lives in your fingers by chapter 5.

<Example title="A 3-D example — the arithmetic still works">

Let `a = (1, 2, 2)` and `b = (2, 0, 1)`.

- Dot product: `a · b = 1·2 + 2·0 + 2·1 = 4`.
- `‖a‖ = √(1 + 4 + 4) = √9 = 3`.
- `‖b‖ = √(4 + 0 + 1) = √5 ≈ 2.236`.
- Cosine similarity: `4 / (3 · √5) = 4 / (3 · 2.236) ≈ 0.596`.
- Angle: `θ = arccos(0.596) ≈ 53.4°`.

Nothing changed except the number of terms. In 100 dimensions there'd be 100 terms in the sum and a 100-term sum-of-squares in each norm. That's still a millisecond of NumPy. This is why the same operation scales from a physics textbook to a trillion-parameter language model.

</Example>

## Projection — extracting the shadow itself

Sometimes you want more than the scalar shadow — you want the actual **vector projection**, the arrow lying along `b` that represents "the part of `a` that goes in `b`'s direction":

$$
\text{proj}_\mathbf{b}(\mathbf{a}) = \left( \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{\mathbf{b} \cdot \mathbf{b}} \right) \mathbf{b}
$$

Parse it slowly. The numerator `a · b` is the scalar dot product. The denominator `b · b` is `‖b‖²`. So the fraction is a scalar; multiplying it by `b` gives a vector along `b`, sized exactly so it lands where the shadow of `a` falls.

<RunnableCode title="scalar and vector projections" code={projectionCode} />

Projection is the backbone of an enormous amount of ML: least-squares regression is literally "project the target vector onto the column space of the feature matrix," Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization is repeated projection, and PCA finds the directions along which projections capture the most variance. If you understand this one formula, you share vocabulary with half of statistics.

## The traps that matter

**Cosine ignores magnitude — by design.** `(4, 4)` and `(400, 400)` have identical cosine similarity to any vector. Usually a feature: long and short documents compare on equal footing. But sometimes magnitude *is* signal — for TF-IDF text vectors, longer matching documents genuinely deserve more weight, which is why BM25 (a length-aware refinement) exists. Choose the metric to match the semantics.

**Cosine is not a distance.** Distances must obey the triangle inequality; cosine similarity — and even `1 − cos θ` — doesn't in general. Vector databases work with cosine on *unit-normalized* vectors, where the space behaves better; treat that as an engineering convention, not a mathematical guarantee.

**High dimensions collapse toward 0.** In hundreds or thousands of dimensions, two *random* vectors are almost always nearly perpendicular — cosine similarities cluster tightly around 0, with spread shrinking like `1/√D`:

<RunnableCode title="Cosine collapse in high dimensions" code={highDimCode} />

At D=1000 the spread is under 0.03. This is why raw cosine values from LLM embedding models can look unimpressive (0.5–0.7) even for very related documents — the useful signal is the *rank order* of scores, not their absolute size. Never judge "is 0.35 good?" without knowing the distribution in your embedding space.

**Zero vectors break the formula** (division by zero). Filter them out or add a small ε to the denominator — remember this the first time a query embedding comes out all-zeros.

**Sign carries meaning.** Negative cosine means "actively opposite," and in modern embedding spaces negatives are rare but meaningful. In classical bag-of-words spaces where all features are non-negative, cosine always lands in [0, 1] and you'll never see a negative.

<RealWorldML title="Transformer attention — the dot product in disguise">

The same operation, dressed as the mechanism inside every modern language model. Attention starts with one vector per token and asks: *for each token, how much should it look at each other token?* The answer is a scaled dot product.

<RunnableCode title="a two-line 'attention' calculation" code={attentionMiniCode} />

Each score is a dot product between the query token (`"dog"`) and every other token. Softmax turns raw scores into a probability distribution — how much attention to pay to each token. The output is the weighted sum of token vectors under those weights. That's what a transformer attention head does, minus (a) the `/ √d_k` scaling for numerical stability and (b) learned matrices producing separate query/key/value vectors. A dot product, a softmax, a weighted sum — that picture repeats in every layer of every LLM, and it's why this chapter earns its depth: the dot product is the *only* operation you must understand deeply to see how a transformer thinks. Everything else is bookkeeping.

</RealWorldML>
