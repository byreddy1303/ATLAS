export const projectionCode = `import numpy as np

def project(v, u):
    """Vector projection of v onto u — the shadow of v on u's direction."""
    return (v @ u) / (u @ u) * u

v = np.array([4.0, 3.0])
u = np.array([1.0, 0.0])          # the x-axis direction

p = project(v, u)                  # shadow of v on the x-axis
r = v - p                          # residual: what's left over — perpendicular to u

print("projection p    =", p)     # (4, 0) — the x-component
print("residual   r    =", r)     # (0, 3) — the y-component
print("p . r          =", round(float(p @ r), 10), " (== 0 -> perpendicular)")
print("check: p + r == v ->", np.allclose(p + r, v))`;

export const gramSchmidtCode = `import numpy as np

def gram_schmidt(vectors):
    """Turn any basis into an orthonormal basis — subtract shadows, then normalize."""
    out = []
    for v in vectors:
        w = v.copy()
        for u in out:                # subtract off shadows onto previously-fixed directions
            w = w - (w @ u) * u      # (u is already unit, so no /(u@u) needed)
        n = np.linalg.norm(w)
        if n > 1e-12:                # skip near-zero vectors (linearly dependent input)
            out.append(w / n)        # normalize to unit length
    return np.stack(out)

V = np.array([[1.0, 1.0, 0.0],
              [1.0, 0.0, 1.0],
              [0.0, 1.0, 1.0]])
Q = gram_schmidt(V)
print("orthonormal basis (rows):")
print(np.round(Q, 3))
print()
print("Q Q^T =")                    # should be the identity
print(np.round(Q @ Q.T, 3))`;

<Motivation title="Least squares, attention, PCA — all the same move">

Here's a fun realization: four totally different-looking ML operations are secretly the same geometric move.

- **Linear regression** fits a line by minimizing squared error. That turns out to be a **projection** of the target onto the space spanned by your features.
- **PCA** (Principal Component Analysis, a way to reduce data to fewer dimensions) finds the direction that captures the most variance. That's a projection whose leftover residual is smallest.
- **Attention** (the transformer building block) averages "value" vectors weighted by attention scores. That's a projection onto the span of the values.
- **Layer normalization** subtracts the mean from a vector's entries. That's a projection onto the subspace where entries sum to zero.

Four different-looking operations, one geometric shape: **drop a perpendicular from a vector onto a subspace, splitting it into the part inside the subspace + a leftover part perpendicular to it.**

Vocabulary you'll need:

- **Projection** = "the closest point in the subspace to your vector."
- **Orthogonal** = another word for **perpendicular** ("makes a 90° angle").
- **Subspace** = a flat "slab" through the origin — could be a line, a plane, or higher-dimensional.

The math for all of this comes from a single identity: two vectors are perpendicular exactly when their dot product is zero. Everything below is turning that one fact into machinery.

The specific formulas take a few lines. The mental picture — the *shadow* on a line, the *residual* perpendicular to it — is what makes least squares stop feeling like a matrix formula and start feeling like a right angle you can draw. That picture is the payoff of this chapter; the derivations below earn it once, so you never have to guess again.

</Motivation>

<Intuition title="Sunlight from directly above">

Point a flashlight straight down at a stick lying on the ground. The **shadow** the stick casts is the *projection* of the stick onto the ground. Two things happen in the picture, and both are exactly what mathematical projection does:

- **The shadow lives on the ground** — it's the closest ground-point-to-the-stick.
- **The stick minus the shadow rises straight up** — perpendicular to the ground.

Every projection in this chapter is that split. Split the vector v into two pieces: the piece **that lies in the target direction (or subspace)**, and the piece **left over, perpendicular to it**. The first piece is the projection; the second is the residual. And the residual is *always* perpendicular to what you projected onto — the shadow can't have a component pointing further along the ground, because you already extracted every drop of "along the ground" into the shadow.

</Intuition>

<Recall term="Dot product zero = perpendicular">

From two chapters ago: a · b = ‖a‖ · ‖b‖ · cos θ, so a · b = 0 exactly when cos θ = 0, i.e. θ = 90°. **Orthogonal** and **perpendicular** are the same word — one geometric, one from Greek. The whole chapter runs on this: "u is perpendicular to v" is not something you draw and squint at, it's the algebra check `u @ v == 0`.

</Recall>

## Orthogonality — the ninety-degree relation

Two vectors are **orthogonal** when their dot product is zero:

$$
\mathbf{u} \perp \mathbf{v} \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0
$$

Three quick consequences that get quoted constantly:

- **The zero vector is orthogonal to everything** (0 · v = 0 for all v).
- **A vector orthogonal to itself is the zero vector** (v · v = ‖v‖² = 0 only if v = 0).
- **A basis whose vectors are pairwise orthogonal** is called an **orthogonal basis**. If additionally every basis vector has length 1, it's **orthonormal** — the format of choice, because it makes coordinates trivial (proved below).

And the geometric bonus: **Pythagoras generalizes**. If a ⊥ b, then

$$
\lVert \mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b} \rVert^2 = \lVert \mathbf{a} \rVert^2 + \lVert \mathbf{b} \rVert^2
$$

— the ancient right-triangle law, dressed up in vector notation. It's a direct consequence of last chapter's identity ‖a + b‖² = ‖a‖² + ‖b‖² + 2a·b: the last term dies when a·b = 0.

## The projection formula — the shadow of v on u

Given a vector v and a nonzero direction u, the **vector projection** of v onto u is the piece of v pointing along u — its shadow. The formula:

$$
\mathrm{proj}_{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{v}) = \frac{\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{u}}{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{u}}\, \mathbf{u}
$$

Two pieces, two jobs:

- **The scalar coefficient** (v·u)/(u·u) says *how far along u* the shadow extends. If u is a unit vector, u·u = 1 and this simplifies to v·u alone — the **scalar projection**.
- **Multiplying by u** turns that scalar into an actual vector pointing along u — that's the shadow itself.

Everything else — least squares, Gram–Schmidt, easy coordinates — is repeated application of this one formula.

<Derivation title="Deriving the projection formula — from 'perpendicular residual' alone">

We ask for the shadow **p** of v on the direction u, and demand the *only* geometric property that makes it a shadow: **the residual r = v − p is perpendicular to u**.

<Step n={1} math={<span>Since <b>p</b> lies along <b>u</b>: <code>p = c u</code> for some scalar c</span>}>
    The whole point of "projection onto u" is that the answer points along u — nowhere else. So <b>p</b> is some scaling of <b>u</b>; we just need to find the right scalar c. That's the only unknown in the problem.
</Step>

<Step n={2} math={<span>Impose perpendicularity: <code>(v − c u) · u = 0</code></span>}>
    "Shadow" is defined by the perpendicular-residual property — that's what makes it the <em>closest</em> point on u's line to v (see the fine print at the end of the derivation). Dot-product-zero encodes 90°. This one equation determines c.
</Step>

<Step n={3} math={<span>Expand with linearity: <code>v·u − c (u·u) = 0</code></span>}>
    The dot product distributes over subtraction (proved slot-by-slot in the dot-product chapter): (v − c u) · u = v·u − (c u)·u, and the scalar c pulls out: (c u)·u = c (u·u). The equation now has one unknown — a linear equation in c.
</Step>

<Step n={4} math={<span>Solve: <code>c = (v · u) / (u · u)</code>, hence <code>proj_u(v) = ((v·u)/(u·u)) u</code></span>}>
    Divide through by u·u (safe because u ≠ <b>0</b> means u·u = ‖u‖² &gt; 0). Substitute back into p = c u and you have the formula. If u happens to be a <b>unit vector</b>, u·u = 1 and c collapses to v·u — the plain dot product <em>is</em> the coordinate. That's the punchline that makes orthonormal bases magical below.
</Step>

<Step n={5} math={<span>Bonus — the closest point: <code>‖v − p‖ ≤ ‖v − q‖</code> for any other q on u's line</span>}>
    We derived <b>p</b> from perpendicularity, not from minimizing distance — but the two conditions are the same. If <b>q</b> = <b>p</b> + s<b>u</b> is any other point on u's line, then v − q = (v − p) − su, and (v − p) ⊥ u, so by Pythagoras ‖v − q‖² = ‖v − p‖² + s²‖u‖². The extra term is ≥ 0, with equality only when s = 0 — i.e., when <b>q</b> = <b>p</b>. So the projection is <em>literally</em> the closest point on u's line to v. This is the reason least squares works: minimizing squared error <em>is</em> projecting.
</Step>

</Derivation>

<Example title="Working it by hand — one full trace">

Project v = (4, 3) onto u = (1, 0) (the x-axis direction):

- v · u = 4·1 + 3·0 = **4**.
- u · u = 1·1 + 0·0 = **1** (u is a unit vector).
- c = 4/1 = **4**.
- proj_u(v) = 4·(1, 0) = **(4, 0)** — the x-component of v, exactly.
- Residual r = v − p = (4, 3) − (4, 0) = **(0, 3)** — the y-component, purely vertical.
- Perpendicularity check: p · r = 4·0 + 0·3 = **0** ✓.
- Pythagoras check: ‖v‖² = 25, ‖p‖² + ‖r‖² = 16 + 9 = **25** ✓ — right triangle with legs 4 and 3, hypotenuse 5.

Now onto a slanted direction: project v = (4, 3) onto u = (1, 1). u · u = 2, v · u = 7, c = 7/2 = 3.5, so p = 3.5(1, 1) = **(3.5, 3.5)**. Residual r = (4, 3) − (3.5, 3.5) = (0.5, −0.5); check r · u = 0.5 − 0.5 = **0** ✓. Same formula, same geometry — the u doesn't have to align with an axis for the trick to work.

</Example>

## Gram–Schmidt — build an orthonormal basis from any basis

Given a set of independent vectors (say a basis of a subspace), **Gram–Schmidt** produces an orthonormal basis of the same subspace — perpendicular unit vectors, same span. The recipe:

1. **Take v₁**, normalize it: u₁ = v₁/‖v₁‖. (Unit length; first perpendicular direction.)
2. **Take v₂**, subtract off its shadow on u₁: w = v₂ − (v₂ · u₁) u₁. That residual is perpendicular to u₁ (by the projection formula itself). Normalize: u₂ = w/‖w‖.
3. **Take v_k**, subtract off its shadow on *every* u_i already fixed: w = v_k − Σᵢ (v_k · u_i) u_i. Normalize.

Each step "peels off" the components of v_k that were already covered by earlier directions, keeping only what's genuinely new — which by construction is perpendicular to everything before. It's just the projection formula, applied in a loop.

<RunnableCode title="Gram–Schmidt in 10 lines" code={gramSchmidtCode} packages={["numpy"]} />

The `Q @ Q.T` producing the identity matrix is the vector way of saying "every pair of rows is perpendicular, every row has length 1" — the signature of an orthonormal set. Read `(Q @ Q.T)[i, j] = q_i · q_j = δ_ij` (1 on the diagonal, 0 off). This will be the definition of an **orthogonal matrix** in the matrices phase.

## Orthonormal bases — coordinates by dot product

Recall from the last chapter: to find the coordinates of x in a basis with columns B, you solve `Bc = x`. That's an O(d³) operation with an ugly matrix inverse.

**When B is orthonormal, no solving is needed** — the coordinates are just dot products:

$$
\mathbf{x} = \sum_{i=1}^{d} (\mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{q}_i)\, \mathbf{q}_i
$$

Why? Because the projection formula collapses when q_i are unit vectors and pairwise perpendicular. Write x = c₁q₁ + ⋯ + c_dq_d, dot both sides with q_j, and every term except one dies (thanks to q_j · q_i = 0 for i ≠ j): x · q_j = c_j (q_j · q_j) = c_j. Solved. No inverse, no linear system — just one dot product per coordinate.

<RunnableCode title="Orthonormal shortcut: coordinates via dot products" code={easyCoordsCode} packages={["numpy"]} />

This is why orthonormal bases are everywhere: rotations (which preserve orthonormality) are cheap, DCT and Fourier bases are orthonormal (making coefficients ⟨signal, basis_k⟩), attention often uses orthogonal initializations for stability, and PCA returns an orthonormal basis of principal components — so projecting your data onto the top-k components is just k dot products per sample.

## The traps that matter

**Projection onto a subspace, not just a line.** Everything above shrinks to "shadow on a line". For a general subspace with orthonormal basis q₁, …, q_k, the projection generalizes to the sum of shadows on each direction:

$$
\mathrm{proj}_S(\mathbf{v}) = \sum_{i=1}^{k} (\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{q}_i)\, \mathbf{q}_i
$$

That's the formula least squares uses (S = column space of the design matrix X), that PCA uses (S = top-k principal components), that image compression uses (S = k basis images). One shape, many jobs — but only clean when the basis is *orthonormal*, which is why Gram–Schmidt exists.

**Numerical Gram–Schmidt bleeds precision.** The classical version above accumulates rounding error and can produce basis vectors that aren't quite orthogonal — for near-parallel input vectors, the residuals become tiny and the normalization amplifies noise. Real libraries use **modified Gram–Schmidt** (interleave the subtractions) or, better, a **QR decomposition** via Householder reflections (`np.linalg.qr(V)`) that's stable at any conditioning. Rule of thumb: understand Gram–Schmidt, call QR.

**"Orthogonal" ≠ "independent" in general — but orthogonal implies independent.** Any set of nonzero pairwise-orthogonal vectors is automatically linearly independent (dot the "sum = 0" relation with each q_j to force all coefficients zero, using the same argument as the coordinates derivation above). The reverse doesn't hold — independent vectors need not be orthogonal — but you can *make* them orthogonal via Gram–Schmidt. So "linearly independent" is the loose condition, "orthonormal" is the deluxe upgrade.

**Least squares IS a projection.** If a linear system `Xw = y` has no solution (more rows than columns, y not in the column space of X), the least-squares solution `ŵ = argmin ‖Xw − y‖` gives you `Xŵ` = projection of y onto span(columns of X). The **normal equations** `XᵀX w = Xᵀy` are literally "the residual y − Xw is orthogonal to every column of X" written matrix-style — same perpendicularity condition, one dimension per column.

Every scientific-computing course fits a line to points; here it is, told as a projection.

<RunnableCode title="Least squares as a projection onto a plane" code={lstsqCode} packages={["numpy", "matplotlib"]} captureFig={true} />

Read the cell:

1. **The design matrix.** `X = [x, ones]` has two columns — one for the slope-coefficient, one for the intercept. Its column space (span of those two columns) is a plane inside ℝ³⁰: every vector of the form `w₁·x + w₂·1` for some (w₁, w₂) — every possible "y = w₁ x + w₂" line evaluated at these 30 x-values.
2. **What lstsq does.** The noisy y vector lives in ℝ³⁰ but almost certainly *outside* that plane (no straight line fits noisy data exactly). Least squares finds the closest point in the plane to y — the **projection** — and returns the (w₁, w₂) that produce it. `y_hat = X @ w` is that projection.
3. **The residual is perpendicular to the column space.** `X.T @ resid ≈ 0` — every column of X (the two directions defining the plane) is orthogonal to the residual. That's step 5 of the derivation, generalized to a plane: the closest point property is equivalent to orthogonal residual, and it's exactly what `lstsq` computes.
4. **The picture.** The gray vertical segments in the plot are the residuals — the vertical distance from each point to the fitted line. Least squares minimizes the sum of *their squared lengths*: ‖y − ŷ‖² — the squared distance in ℝ³⁰ from y to its projection onto the plane. Squared distance, minimum, projection: the three names for the same operation.

The same skeleton — build a design matrix, solve `lstsq` — fits polynomials, exponentials, and neural network output layers. The geometry never changes: it's always "project the target onto the span of the features."

<Summary>

- **Orthogonal = perpendicular = dot product zero**. Pythagoras generalizes: if a ⊥ b, ‖a + b‖² = ‖a‖² + ‖b‖².
- **Projection formula**: `proj_u(v) = ((v·u)/(u·u)) u` — the shadow of v on u. For unit u it simplifies to `(v·u) u`.
- The split every projection produces: v = (shadow on u) + (residual perpendicular to u); the shadow is also the *closest point* on u's line to v.
- **Gram–Schmidt** turns any independent set into an orthonormal one by subtracting off shadows on already-fixed directions, then normalizing. Numerically prefer `np.linalg.qr`.
- **Orthonormal basis magic**: coordinates are dot products, cᵢ = x · qᵢ — no linear-system solve needed. The reason PCA, DCT, Fourier, and orthogonal-init neural nets are all cheap.
- **Least squares IS a projection**: minimizing ‖Xw − y‖ projects y onto span(cols of X); the normal equations `XᵀX w = Xᵀy` are the "residual perpendicular to every column" condition.

</Summary>
