export const distanceCode = `import numpy as np

a = np.array([1.0, 2.0])
b = np.array([4.0, 6.0])
diff = a - b                       # distance = the norm of the displacement

print("Euclidean =", np.linalg.norm(diff))           # 5.0 (crow flies)
print("Manhattan =", np.linalg.norm(diff, 1))        # 7.0 (grid streets)
print("Chebyshev =", np.linalg.norm(diff, np.inf))   # 4.0 (king moves)

# the identity that powers vector databases:
#   ||a - b||^2 = ||a||^2 + ||b||^2 - 2 a.b
lhs = np.linalg.norm(a - b) ** 2
rhs = np.linalg.norm(a) ** 2 + np.linalg.norm(b) ** 2 - 2 * (a @ b)
print("identity:", round(float(lhs), 2), "==", round(float(rhs), 2))`;

export const normalizeCode = `import numpy as np

def normalize(v):
    n = np.linalg.norm(v)
    return v / n if n > 0 else v    # guard: the zero vector has no direction

a = np.array([3.0, 4.0])
u = normalize(a)
print("unit vector:", u, "  length =", np.linalg.norm(u))

# on unit vectors, the plain dot product IS cosine similarity
b = np.array([1.0, 7.0])
cos = normalize(a) @ normalize(b)
print("cosine similarity =", round(float(cos), 4))

# and Euclidean distance becomes a function of that SAME cosine
d2 = np.linalg.norm(normalize(a) - normalize(b)) ** 2
print("unit distance^2 =", round(float(d2), 4),
      "  == 2 - 2cos =", round(float(2 - 2 * cos), 4))`;

    "sourdough starter guide",
    "fine-tuning transformers on small data",
]

    [0.9, 0.1, 0.8, 0.1],
    [0.1, 0.9, 0.1, 0.7],

<Motivation title="'Closest' is a design decision">

Every RAG system answers one geometric question: *which stored embedding is closest to the query embedding?* But "closest" isn't one thing — it depends on the **ruler**. Measure with the L2 ruler and you get Euclidean distance; with L1, Manhattan distance; rank by cosine and you're not even using a true distance (we'll prove that). Vector databases let you pick, and picking wrong quietly degrades retrieval.

The same letters follow you everywhere else in ML: **L2** appears in weight decay and gradient clipping, **L1** in lasso regularization (it's what creates sparsity — the norm-ball picture below shows why), **L∞** in adversarial robustness ("no pixel changed by more than ε"). The choice of norm is a modeling decision, not a formality.

This chapter gives you the three rulers, one identity that welds distance to the dot product (the identity vector databases are built on), and the traps — including why "cosine distance" breaks the rules real distances obey.

</Motivation>

<Intuition title="Three rulers for the same walk">

A norm answers one question: **how long is this walk?** The same displacement (3, −4) has three honest answers, depending on how you're allowed to move. The **crow** flies straight: length 5 (L2). The **taxi** is stuck on a street grid, driving 3 blocks then 4: length 7 (L1). The **chess king** moves diagonally as easily as straight, so he needs max(3, 4) = 4 moves (L∞). Same walk, three rulers. And distance is never a new idea: the distance from a to b is just the *length of the displacement walk* a − b, measured with whichever ruler you chose.

</Intuition>

<Recall term="Norm from the dot product">

From the dot-product chapter: a vector dotted with itself gives the sum of its squared entries, v · v = v₁² + … + vₙ², so the (L2) length is ‖v‖ = √(v·v). That one link — length via dot product — does all the heavy lifting in today's derivation.

</Recall>

## The L2 norm — the ruler you already know

The **Euclidean norm** is Pythagoras, run in any number of dimensions:

$$
\lVert \mathbf{v} \rVert_2 = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2 + \cdots + v_n^2} = \sqrt{\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v}}
$$

It's the default: when a paper writes ‖v‖ with no subscript, it means L2. Three properties make any norm a legitimate ruler, and L2 has them all:

1. **Non-negative, and zero only for the zero vector** — every real walk has positive length.
2. **Homogeneity**: ‖c v‖ = |c| · ‖v‖ — triple the stride, triple the length (the |·| is why −2v is *twice* as long, not "−2 times").
3. **Triangle inequality**: ‖a + b‖ ≤ ‖a‖ + ‖b‖ — a detour is never shorter than the straight walk.

A vector with ‖u‖ = 1 is a **unit vector** — pure direction, no magnitude. Dividing any nonzero v by its own norm produces one: u = v/‖v‖ (homogeneity guarantees length 1). This is what "normalizing an embedding" means, and it's why cosine similarity is just a dot product of normalized vectors.

## L1 and L∞ — two more rulers

Two more norms cover most of ML. The **L1 norm** (taxicab/Manhattan) sums absolute entries; the **L∞ norm** (max/Chebyshev) takes the single largest:

$$
\lVert \mathbf{v} \rVert_1 = |v_1| + \cdots + |v_n|
\qquad\qquad
\lVert \mathbf{v} \rVert_\infty = \max_i |v_i|
$$

Both satisfy the same three ruler laws. Where each shows up:

- **L1** — lasso regularization (pushes weights to exactly zero → sparse models), and error metrics robust to outliers (MAE).
- **L2** — everywhere by default: distances, weight decay, gradient clipping.
- **L∞** — "worst single coordinate": adversarial attacks bounded by ε per pixel, sanity checks like "is any gradient entry exploding?".

For every vector, the three answers are ordered: ‖v‖∞ ≤ ‖v‖₂ ≤ ‖v‖₁ — the king's count never exceeds the crow's, the crow never exceeds the taxi.

## Distance is the norm of the displacement

No new definitions needed. Last chapter established that q − p is *the arrow from p to q*; a distance is that arrow's length under your chosen ruler:

$$
d(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}) = \lVert \mathbf{a} - \mathbf{b} \rVert
$$

L2 gives **Euclidean** distance, L1 gives **Manhattan**, L∞ gives **Chebyshev**. Because they're built from norms, all three obey the metric laws you'd demand of any honest distance: d(a, b) = 0 exactly when a = b, symmetry d(a, b) = d(b, a), and the triangle inequality d(a, c) ≤ d(a, b) + d(b, c) — no shortcut through a midpoint can beat the direct route. Hold onto that last law: it's the one "cosine distance" fails.

<Derivation title="‖a − b‖² = ‖a‖² + ‖b‖² − 2 a·b — welding distance to the dot product">

Everything comes from one recalled fact — ‖v‖² = v·v — plus the algebra laws the dot product obeys (it distributes over addition and commutes, both proved slot-by-slot in its chapter).

<Step n={1} math={<span><code>‖a − b‖² = (a − b) · (a − b)</code></span>}>
    Apply the recalled fact to the vector v = a − b: its squared length is itself dotted with itself. No geometry yet — just the definition.
</Step>

<Step n={2} math={<span>Expand like algebra: <code>(a − b)·(a − b) = a·a − a·b − b·a + b·b</code></span>}>
    The dot product distributes over addition and subtraction exactly like multiplication over numbers, so FOIL works: four terms, each a dot product. This "multiply out the brackets" move is legal *because* distributivity was proved — it's not an analogy.
</Step>

<Step n={3} math={<span>Commute and collect: <code>‖a − b‖² = ‖a‖² + ‖b‖² − 2 a·b</code></span>}>
    a·a is ‖a‖², b·b is ‖b‖², and a·b = b·a lets the two middle terms merge into −2a·b. Read the result: <em>squared distance is squared lengths minus twice the similarity</em>. The more aligned two vectors are (bigger dot product), the closer they are — distance and similarity are two views of one quantity.
</Step>

<Step n={4} math={<span>Unit vectors: <code>‖u − w‖² = 2 − 2 cos θ</code></span>}>
    If both vectors are normalized, ‖u‖² = ‖w‖² = 1 and u·w = cos θ, so squared distance is 2 − 2cos θ. Consequence: on normalized embeddings, <b>smallest Euclidean distance and largest cosine similarity pick the same winner</b> — d² falls exactly when cos rises. That's why vector databases can offer "cosine" and "euclidean" modes and return identical rankings once you normalize — and why they often just compute the dot product, the cheapest of the three.
</Step>

</Derivation>

<Example title="Working it by hand — one full trace">

Take v = (3, −4):

- **L1**: |3| + |−4| = **7** — the taxi drives 3 blocks, then 4.
- **L2**: √(3² + (−4)²) = √25 = **5** — the crow's diagonal.
- **L∞**: max(|3|, |−4|) = **4** — the king needs 4 moves (3 of them diagonal).
- Ordering check: 4 ≤ 5 ≤ 7 ✓.

Now a distance. For a = (1, 2) and b = (4, 6): the displacement is a − b = (−3, −4) — the same numbers, so Euclidean distance 5, Manhattan 7, Chebyshev 4. Signs never matter to a ruler: ‖−v‖ = ‖v‖ by homogeneity (|−1| = 1), which is the symmetry d(a, b) = d(b, a) in action. And in ℝ¹²²⁸⁸ the recipe is identical — square, sum, root, just with more slots.

</Example>

## Norm balls — what "distance 1" looks like

Draw every point whose norm is exactly 1 and each ruler reveals its shape: L2 gives the familiar **circle**, L1 a **diamond** (its corners sit on the axes), L∞ a **square**. One picture is worth the three definitions:

The shapes explain behavior. **Why L1 regularization creates sparsity**: penalizing ‖w‖₁ pulls weights toward a diamond, and a diamond's *corners* — where some coordinates are exactly zero — are where solutions land; the circle of L2 has no corners, so weights shrink smoothly but rarely hit zero exactly. You'll meet this argument again, with the full picture, in the regularization chapter — the geometry is already complete here.

## The traps that matter

**"Cosine distance" is not a metric.** The quantity 1 − cos θ is a fine *ranking score* but violates the triangle inequality — two small "distances" can chain into a large one (a problem below has you build the counterexample with three simple vectors). Consequence: algorithms whose correctness assumes a true metric (some tree-based neighbor searches, some clustering guarantees) can silently misbehave with it. Rule of thumb: rank with cosine, but if a method demands a metric, normalize and use Euclidean — step 4 of the derivation says the ranking survives.

**High dimensions squeeze distances together.** For high-dimensional data, pairwise Euclidean distances *concentrate*: the nearest and farthest neighbors can differ by only a few percent, making raw distance less informative. This is one face of the curse of dimensionality, and one reason embeddings are compared with cosine (after normalizing away magnitude) rather than raw L2 — direction stays meaningful when lengths stop being so.

**Norms add apples and oranges.** A distance over features on different scales is dominated by the biggest unit: with (age in years, income in dollars), income differences of hundreds crush age differences of decades. Standardize features (subtract mean, divide by standard deviation, per column) before any distance-based method — kNN, k-means, retrieval over hand-built features. A problem below shows a nearest-neighbor *ranking flip* caused purely by units.

**Normalizing the zero vector.** v/‖v‖ divides by zero when v = 0 — the zero vector has no direction to extract. Real pipelines guard it (`if n > 0`) or add a tiny ε. This bites in practice: a padding token's all-zero embedding, an empty document, a ReLU layer that zeroed everything.

Read the cell:

<Summary>

- A norm is a **ruler**: L1 sums |entries| (taxi), L2 is Pythagoras (crow, the default), L∞ takes the max |entry| (king). Always ‖v‖∞ ≤ ‖v‖₂ ≤ ‖v‖₁.
- Every norm obeys three laws: positive (zero only at **0**), homogeneous (‖cv‖ = |c|‖v‖), triangle inequality (detours never win).
- **Distance = ‖a − b‖** — the length of the displacement, under whichever ruler you picked: Euclidean / Manhattan / Chebyshev.
- THE identity: ‖a − b‖² = ‖a‖² + ‖b‖² − 2a·b. On unit vectors, d² = 2 − 2cos θ — so after normalizing, Euclidean ranking ≡ cosine ranking ≡ dot-product ranking.
- Unit vector: u = v/‖v‖ (guard v = 0). "Normalize your embeddings" means exactly this — then cosine similarity is one matmul.
- Traps: cosine "distance" breaks the triangle inequality (rank with it, don't treat it as a metric); high-dim distances concentrate; standardize features before distance-based methods.

</Summary>
