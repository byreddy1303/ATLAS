export const varianceCode = `# Variance = E[(X - E[X])^2] = "average squared distance from the mean"
import numpy as np

# Two distributions with the SAME mean but different spreads
mean = 5.0
concentrated = np.array([4, 5, 5, 5, 6])      # tight around 5
spread       = np.array([1, 3, 5, 7, 9])      # wider

for name, arr in [("concentrated", concentrated), ("spread", spread)]:
    mu = arr.mean()
    var = ((arr - mu) ** 2).mean()
    std = np.sqrt(var)
    print(f"{name:>13}: mean={mu}, variance={var:.2f}, std={std:.3f}")

# The identity: Var(X) = E[X^2] - E[X]^2 (short-cut form)
X = spread
short_cut = (X ** 2).mean() - X.mean() ** 2
print(f"\\nVar(spread) via short-cut identity: {short_cut}")
print(f"Compare to direct  E[(X-mu)^2]     : {((X - X.mean()) ** 2).mean()}")

# NumPy's np.var uses N in the denominator by default (population variance)
# Sample variance uses N-1 (Bessel's correction) — unbiased estimator
print(f"\\nnp.var(X) [population]: {np.var(X)}")
print(f"np.var(X, ddof=1) [sample]: {np.var(X, ddof=1)}")`;

<Motivation title="Expectation is 'what you'd get on average'; variance is 'how much it wobbles'">

You've been computing averages since middle school. **Expectation** is just a fancy math word for "the average, weighted by probability." Every quantity in ML that "averages over something" is really an expectation:

- **Mean squared error loss** averages the squared prediction errors.
- **Log-likelihood** (from the estimation chapter) averages log probabilities.
- **Entropy** averages −log p (uncertainty of a distribution).
- **KL divergence** averages log(p/q) (how different two distributions are).

Every "sample many, take the mean" trick — every SGD step, every Monte Carlo estimate, every RL policy evaluation — is standing on this idea.

But the mean is only half the story. You also need to know **how much your quantity wobbles around that mean**. A model that gets 87% accuracy on average is very different from one that gets 87% ± 2% versus 87% ± 20%. That's what **variance** captures — the spread around the mean.

Together, expectation and variance are the minimum vocabulary for reasoning about anything probabilistic in ML: the model's outputs, batch-averaged losses, gradient noise, evaluation error bars.

For a GenAI engineer, this chapter unlocks:

- **Loss decomposition**: MSE = squared bias + variance. This is the famous **bias-variance tradeoff** of classical ML (whole chapter on it in Tier 2).
- **Gradient noise**: SGD's noise variance depends on batch size. Smaller batches → noisier gradients → more implicit regularization from the wobble.
- **Sample complexity**: to estimate an average precisely, you need enough samples that the standard error (= standard deviation / √N) is small enough for your use case.
- **Linearity of expectation**: even when random variables aren't independent, their expected values add cleanly. Huge algebraic shortcut.

The formulas are simple — one sum. The mental picture is what really pays off.

</Motivation>

<Intuition title="Expectation is the balance point; variance is how far things scatter">

Here's a picture that makes both concepts concrete.

Imagine you spread little weights along a number line, one weight per possible outcome, sized in proportion to how likely each outcome is. So a fair die would put six equal weights at positions 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6. A loaded die would put bigger weights where the probabilities are higher.

**The expectation E[X] is the balance point** — the spot where you'd put a pivot to keep the line balanced. For symmetric distributions (like a fair die), this is the middle. For skewed ones, it's dragged toward the heavier side.

**Variance Var(X) is "how spread out the weights are from the balance point."** Weights all clumped near the pivot → low variance (predictable). Weights scattered far to the sides → high variance (wildly unpredictable).

**Standard deviation σ = √Var** is variance in the original units — much easier to interpret. "Outputs are 5 ± 2" makes intuitive sense. "5 with variance 4" is the same thing, but harder to picture. Always convert to standard deviation when talking to humans.

Both are single-number summaries. Two distributions can have the same mean but wildly different variances — one is predictable, the other is a rollercoaster. Reporting a mean without a standard deviation is often misleading.

</Intuition>

<Recall term="Random variable">

A random variable X is a function from the sample space to numbers — for each outcome, it assigns a numerical value. Its distribution is fully described by either the PMF (probability mass function, discrete case) or PDF (probability density function, continuous case). Expectation, variance, and all other "moments" are summaries computed from that distribution.

</Recall>

## Expectation — the weighted average

For a discrete random variable X taking values x_1, x_2, ..., x_K with probabilities p_1, p_2, ..., p_K:

$$
E[X] \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{K} p_i \cdot x_i
$$

For a continuous random variable with density f(x):

$$
E[X] \;=\; \int x \cdot f(x) \, dx
$$

Both are "value weighted by probability, summed" — the continuous version just uses integration. E[X] is the number you'd get on average from infinitely many independent samples of X (the law of large numbers).

**Key property**: for a function g of X, E[g(X)] = Σ p_i · g(x_i). "Take g of the outcome, weight by probability, sum." This is how variance is defined (g(x) = (x − μ)²), how entropy is defined (g(x) = -log p(x)), and so on.

## Variance — the average squared distance from the mean

The **variance** of X is the expected value of the squared deviation from the mean:

$$
\mathrm{Var}(X) \;=\; E\big[(X - E[X])^2\big] \;=\; E[X^2] - E[X]^2
$$

The second form (E[X²] − E[X]²) is the "short-cut" computational form, useful when you can compute both E[X²] and E[X] easily. Prove it by expanding (X − μ)² = X² − 2μX + μ² and taking expectation, using linearity.

**Standard deviation** σ = √Var is variance in the original units. For a Gaussian distribution N(μ, σ²), about 68% of samples fall within one σ of the mean, 95% within 2σ, 99.7% within 3σ. This "68-95-99.7 rule" is why standard deviation is so widely used — it's the natural scale for "typical spread."

## Linearity of expectation — the theorem that pays for itself constantly

**Linearity of expectation** is a deeply useful property:

$$
E[aX + bY + c] \;=\; a \cdot E[X] + b \cdot E[Y] + c
$$

for any constants a, b, c and any random variables X, Y — **including when X and Y are NOT independent**. This is the identity that lets you compute expectations of complicated sums by breaking them into simple pieces.

Contrast with variance, which is **not** linear (even for independent variables):

$$
\mathrm{Var}(aX + bY) \;=\; a^2 \mathrm{Var}(X) + b^2 \mathrm{Var}(Y) + 2ab \cdot \mathrm{Cov}(X, Y)
$$

The covariance term is what breaks linearity. For **independent** X and Y, Cov(X, Y) = 0, so Var(X + Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y) — variance is *additive for independent variables* but not in general.

## Covariance and correlation

**Covariance** measures how much two random variables vary together:

$$
\mathrm{Cov}(X, Y) \;=\; E[(X - E[X])(Y - E[Y])] \;=\; E[XY] - E[X] E[Y]
$$

- Positive covariance: X and Y tend to be above/below their means together.
- Negative covariance: X and Y tend to be on opposite sides of their means.
- Zero covariance: X and Y are "uncorrelated" (no linear relationship).

Covariance's magnitude depends on the units of X and Y — hard to interpret directly. **Correlation** is the dimensionless version:

$$
\rho_{XY} \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{Cov}(X, Y)}{\sigma_X \cdot \sigma_Y} \;\in\; [-1, +1]
$$

Correlation ranges from −1 (perfectly anti-linear) to +1 (perfectly linear), with 0 meaning "no linear relationship." This is what `np.corrcoef` and `pandas.DataFrame.corr` compute.

**The trap**: uncorrelated does NOT mean independent. Correlation only measures *linear* dependence. Two variables can be perfectly deterministically related (e.g., Y = X² for X centered at 0) and still have correlation 0. This limitation is why we sometimes need mutual information (from the entropy chapter) instead — it captures non-linear dependence too.

## Things that trip people up

**"E[g(X)] is not the same as g(E[X])."** You can't just plug the mean into a nonlinear function and expect the right answer. In fact, **Jensen's inequality** tells you which direction it goes for a convex g: E[g(X)] ≥ g(E[X]). (Convex just means "curves upward, like a bowl." Squaring, exponential, negative log are all convex.) Practical consequence: "the expected loss of the mean prediction" is *not* the same as "the mean of the per-sample losses." You have to average the loss over samples.

**"Variance doesn't add unless you're independent."** Var(X + Y) ≠ Var(X) + Var(Y) in general. There's an extra term called covariance that has to be included:

$$\mathrm{Var}(X+Y) = \mathrm{Var}(X) + \mathrm{Var}(Y) + 2\,\mathrm{Cov}(X, Y).$$

For **independent** X and Y (or even just **uncorrelated** ones), Cov(X, Y) = 0 and the extra term vanishes — but independence assumptions are often wrong in real data.

**"Sample variance uses N − 1, not N."** This is called **Bessel's correction**. The math reason: because you used the sample mean itself to compute the deviations, one "degree of freedom" is spent, so only N − 1 residuals are free to vary. Dividing by N − 1 gives an unbiased estimator. In NumPy: `np.var(x, ddof=1)` uses N − 1 (correct); `np.var(x)` uses N (biased-low). For large N the difference is negligible.

**"Uncorrelated does not mean independent."** The classic counterexample: X uniform on [−1, 1] and Y = X². By symmetry, Corr(X, Y) = 0 — but Y is *completely determined* by X. They're not independent at all. Correlation only sees the linear part of a relationship. For fully nonlinear dependence, you need **mutual information** (covered in the entropy chapter).

**"Higher moments exist but usually don't matter."** The third **central moment** measures **skewness** (is the distribution lopsided?), the fourth measures **kurtosis** (are the tails heavy?). Rarely used directly in mainstream ML — mostly show up in finance and risk modeling.

**"Averaging a random model gives you Bayesian inference."** If your model f is itself uncertain (say, you have multiple training runs, or a Bayesian neural network), then E[f(x)] averaged over the model's uncertainty is a natural best prediction. This is exactly what **deep ensembles** and MC Dropout compute.

<RealWorldML title="Mean squared error decomposes into bias-squared plus variance">

The bias-variance decomposition is one of the most useful theorems in ML. For any predictor f(x) trying to predict a true value y:

$$
E[(f(x) - y)^2] \;=\; \big(E[f(x)] - y\big)^2 + \mathrm{Var}[f(x)]
$$

**MSE = Bias² + Variance**. Where the randomness is over: dataset draws (if f is trained on random data), stochastic training, or ensemble members.

- **Bias** (E[f(x)] − y): how far, on average, the predictor's mean prediction is from the true value. Systematic error.
- **Variance** (Var[f(x)]): how much the predictor's output would vary if we retrained on different random data or added noise. Random error.

**The tradeoff**: complex models (deep nets, high-degree polynomials) can achieve low bias (they fit the training data closely) but often have high variance (small changes in the data lead to big changes in the model). Simple models (linear regression, decision stumps) have low variance (predictions are stable) but often high bias (they can't fit complex patterns).

In modern deep learning, the situation is subtler — big models with lots of data can achieve both low bias AND low variance (the "double descent" phenomenon), which is why we can train billion-parameter models without catastrophic overfitting.

**Practical use**: when your test error is high, ask "is it because of bias (model too simple) or variance (model too complex, overfitting)?" Diagnostic: if training error is also high → bias problem (need bigger model or better features). If training error is low but test error is high → variance problem (need more data, more regularization, or a simpler model).

This chapter provides the mathematical scaffolding: MSE decomposes as (E[error])² + Var(error), and each term is measurable, tunable, and diagnostic.

</RealWorldML>

<CodeItYourself title="Three drills — mean/variance, linearity, sample-size effects">

1. **Sample vs population variance.** Draw N samples from N(0, 1) for N = 5, 30, 300. For each, compute variance both ways (with N and N-1 in denominator) and compare to true variance (1.0). Which is closer? For small N, the N-1 version is unbiased (correct on average); the N version underestimates.

2. **Verify linearity of expectation.** Generate two dependent random variables (e.g., X ~ N(0, 1) and Y = X + N(0, 0.5)). Sample 10,000 times. Verify that sample_mean(X + Y) ≈ sample_mean(X) + sample_mean(Y), even though X and Y are correlated. Then verify that Var(X + Y) ≠ Var(X) + Var(Y) — you need the 2·Cov(X, Y) correction.

3. **Standard error of the mean.** Estimate the mean of a distribution by averaging N samples. Plot the standard deviation of the sample mean as N ranges from 10 to 10,000 (log scale). It should scale as 1/√N — a fundamental relationship that tells you how many samples you need to reduce estimation error by a target factor.
