# a Gaussian shape, regardless of the underlying distribution -- for large enough N.
# Demonstrate by averaging draws from very non-Gaussian distributions.
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# Compute sample means for three quite different underlying distributions.
distributions = {
    "Uniform(0, 1)":      lambda size: rng.uniform(0, 1, size=size),
    "Exponential(rate=1)": lambda size: rng.exponential(1.0, size=size),
    "Bernoulli(p=0.1)":    lambda size: (rng.random(size=size) < 0.1).astype(float),
}

n_averages = 5000    # how many sample means to compute
n_per_avg  = 200     # how many draws per sample mean

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(11, 3.4), facecolor="none")
for ax, (name, sampler) in zip(axes, distributions.items()):
    ax.set_facecolor("#0f0f14")
    # 5000 sample means, each an average of 200 draws.
    samples = sampler((n_averages, n_per_avg))
    means = samples.mean(axis=1)

export const cltAppliedCode = `# Direct engineering use of CLT: constructing a confidence interval
# for the true mean of a population from a single sample.
import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import norm
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# Pretend we don't know the true distribution.  Draw a single sample of size N.
# Population: Exponential(rate=2), so true mean = 0.5, true var = 0.25.
N = 400
sample = rng.exponential(scale=0.5, size=N)   # scale = 1/rate
sample_mean = sample.mean()
sample_std  = sample.std(ddof=1)   # use N-1 denominator for unbiased estimate

# By CLT: sample_mean is approximately N(true_mean, true_var / N).
# We don't know true_var; use sample_std as an estimate.  Standard error:
SE = sample_std / np.sqrt(N)
# 95% CI: sample_mean +- 1.96 * SE.
z95 = norm.ppf(0.975)
ci_low = sample_mean - z95 * SE
ci_high = sample_mean + z95 * SE

print(f"Sample of size {N}: mean = {sample_mean:.4f}, std = {sample_std:.4f}")
print(f"Standard error of the mean: {SE:.4f}")
print(f"95% CI for the true mean: ({ci_low:.4f}, {ci_high:.4f})")
print(f"True population mean = 0.5 -- inside the interval? {ci_low <= 0.5 <= ci_high}")

# Verify: repeat the experiment many times, check coverage.
n_experiments = 2000
inside = 0
for _ in range(n_experiments):
    s = rng.exponential(scale=0.5, size=N)
    m, sd = s.mean(), s.std(ddof=1)
    lo = m - z95 * sd / np.sqrt(N)
    hi = m + z95 * sd / np.sqrt(N)
    if lo <= 0.5 <= hi:
        inside += 1
print(f"\\nEmpirical coverage over {n_experiments} experiments: {inside / n_experiments * 100:.2f}%   (target: 95%)")`;

<Motivation title="Two theorems that explain why averaging works — LLN and CLT">

Two theorems drive almost all of applied probability. Both are simple to state; both are why every ML metric with a "±" behind it exists.

- The **Law of Large Numbers (LLN)** says: as you gather more independent samples, their average gets closer and closer to the true underlying mean. If you flip a fair coin 10 times you might get 7 heads. If you flip it 10,000 times, you'll be very close to 5,000. That "getting closer" is LLN.
- The **Central Limit Theorem (CLT)** says something even wilder: no matter what shape your original data has, the *sample mean* is bell-curve-shaped around the true mean, with standard deviation σ/√N. This is why bell curves show up everywhere — they're the universal shape of averaged noise.

Together, these two theorems justify almost every ML measurement you make:

- **Test-set accuracy** is a sample mean over your test examples. LLN says it converges to the true accuracy as your test set grows. CLT gives you the error bars: SE = √(p(1−p)/N).
- **A/B testing** uses CLT to compute how many users you need and to check whether a 2% observed lift is "3.5 standard deviations away from zero" — solidly significant.
- **SGD gradient estimates** are sample means of per-example gradients. LLN says they converge to the true gradient; CLT says the wobble around it is bell-curve-shaped with variance that shrinks as you make the batch bigger.
- **Monte Carlo integration** — RL policy evaluation, Bayesian posterior estimation, simulation-based inference. All of them estimate quantities as sample means. Error bars come from LLN + CLT.
- **Loss curves that wiggle during training** — those wiggles are CLT-shaped noise on the loss, with size ≈ Var(loss)/√batch_size.

Learn these two theorems once, use them forever. This chapter closes Phase 1.4 and sets up the statistical estimation chapter of Phase 1.5.

</Motivation>

<Intuition title="LLN: averages settle down. CLT: they settle in a bell-shaped cloud that shrinks as 1/√N.">

Two mental pictures — one for each theorem.

**Picture 1 (LLN).** You start flipping a fair coin. After 10 flips you might have 6/10 heads — a decent chunk away from 0.5. After 100 flips you're at 0.51. After 1,000 flips, 0.502. After 10,000 flips, 0.5003. The running fraction is *homing in* on the true probability. Nothing magical is going on — this is just what "sample averages get close to the truth" looks like. LLN says this is *guaranteed* for iid samples with a finite mean.

**Picture 2 (CLT).** Now imagine doing that whole "flip 100 coins, count heads" experiment many times over. Each experiment gives you a slightly different fraction. If you plot the distribution of those fractions, guess what shape you get? A bell curve — specifically, one centered at the true 0.5 with standard deviation σ/√N. Doesn't matter what the original data looks like (uniform, exponential, Poisson, Bernoulli — anything with finite variance) — once you average enough draws, the sample-mean distribution is bell-curve-shaped.

Two takeaways:

1. Averages give you the right answer *eventually* (LLN).
2. The wobble around that answer has a *known shape* (Gaussian) and a *known scale* (σ/√N). So you can put error bars on it (CLT).

Together they tell you not just *what* the answer is but *how confident* you should be about it.

</Intuition>

<Recall term="Sample mean">

The sample mean of X_1, …, X_N is X̄ = (X_1 + X_2 + … + X_N) / N. If the X_i are iid with mean μ and variance σ², then E[X̄] = μ and Var(X̄) = σ²/N. The variance shrinks as 1/N; the standard deviation (called the **standard error of the mean**, SE) shrinks as 1/√N.

</Recall>

## Law of large numbers — sample averages converge to true means

The **weak law of large numbers** states: for iid random variables X_1, X_2, … with finite mean μ = E[X],

$$
\bar X_N \;=\; \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} X_i \;\longrightarrow\; \mu \quad \text{as } N \to \infty,
$$

in *probability*: for every ε &gt; 0, P(|X̄_N − μ| &gt; ε) → 0.

The **strong law of large numbers** upgrades convergence to *almost sure*: the sample path X̄_N converges to μ for almost every possible sequence of iid draws. Both versions require only that E[|X|] &lt; ∞. Finite variance is not needed.

**Proof sketch (weak law via Chebyshev)**. Var(X̄_N) = σ²/N by independence. Chebyshev&rsquo;s inequality says P(|Y − E[Y]| &gt; ε) ≤ Var(Y)/ε². Apply with Y = X̄_N: P(|X̄_N − μ| &gt; ε) ≤ σ²/(N ε²) → 0. ∎

**What LLN does not say**. It doesn&rsquo;t say the sample mean equals μ at any finite N. It doesn&rsquo;t give a rate. It doesn&rsquo;t say anything about the sample&rsquo;s fluctuation shape. Those are the CLT&rsquo;s job.

## Central limit theorem — the shape of the fluctuation

The **classical central limit theorem** states: for iid random variables X_1, X_2, … with finite mean μ and finite variance σ², the standardized sample mean converges in distribution to a standard normal:

$$
Z_N \;=\; \frac{\bar X_N - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{N}} \;\xrightarrow{d}\; \mathcal{N}(0, 1) \quad \text{as } N \to \infty.
$$

Equivalently, X̄_N is *approximately* 𝒩(μ, σ²/N) for large N, and Σ X_i is *approximately* 𝒩(N μ, N σ²).

**Why it works** (intuition, not proof). The Gaussian is the *maximum-entropy* distribution with a fixed mean and variance. When you add many independent contributions, you&rsquo;re maximizing entropy subject to the mean and variance constraints imposed by the fixed E[X] and Var(X) — and the Gaussian is the answer. Every skewness or shape feature of the original distribution gets averaged away.

**Universality**. The CLT holds for uniform, exponential, Poisson, Bernoulli, discrete, continuous, skewed, or symmetric — anything with finite variance. This is why the Gaussian is *the* universal noise model in ML: aggregate measurements are approximately Gaussian regardless of the microstructure.

**How large does N have to be?** For a symmetric-ish distribution (uniform), N = 10 already looks quite Gaussian. For a very skewed one (Exponential, Bernoulli with p far from 0.5), you need N in the hundreds. Heavy-tailed distributions (no finite variance) fail CLT entirely — the sample mean has a *non-Gaussian* limit distribution (α-stable law) or fails to converge at all.

## The three ingredients CLT actually needs

1. **Independence** (or at least weak dependence — variants of CLT extend to some dependent-data settings).
2. **Identical distribution** (or at least Lindeberg-condition style bounded variances — the Lindeberg-Feller CLT relaxes iid to varying variances).
3. **Finite variance** σ² &lt; ∞.

Break any of these and the standard CLT can fail:

- **Heavy tails**: Cauchy, Pareto with tail index α &lt; 2 → infinite variance → different limit theorem (α-stable law), not Gaussian.
- **Strong dependence**: highly autocorrelated time series → effective sample size is much smaller than N → SE is not σ/√N.
- **Non-identical distributions with unbounded variance**: needs Lindeberg-Feller conditions to guarantee Gaussian limit.

For most ML applications, iid + finite variance is a reasonable assumption. Time series, hierarchical data, and streaming settings often need care.

## The 1/√N law — the arithmetic of sample size

CLT says the standard error of the sample mean is σ/√N. Rearranging:

$$
\text{To reduce SE by a factor of } k, \text{ multiply } N \text{ by } k^2.
$$

- To halve the error: 4× samples.
- 10× more precision: 100× samples.
- Two more decimal places of accuracy: 10,000× samples.

This is the "expensive" side of statistical estimation. It&rsquo;s why big-N experiments matter for small effects, why A/B tests can require weeks of traffic to detect 1% lifts, and why SGD gradient estimates need increasing batch sizes for increasingly precise estimates.

## Applied CLT — confidence intervals in one line

Given a sample X_1, …, X_N with sample mean X̄ and sample standard deviation s, a 95% confidence interval for the true mean μ is (approximately, for large N):

$$
\bar X \;\pm\; 1.96 \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{N}}.
$$

The 1.96 is the 97.5th percentile of the standard normal (so 95% of mass lies within ±1.96 σ). Use `norm.ppf(0.975)` in scipy. For small N (say &lt; 30), replace 1.96 with the t-distribution&rsquo;s corresponding percentile (Student&rsquo;s t is a heavy-tailed adjustment for small samples; converges to normal as N → ∞).

**Interpretation.** If you repeat this experiment many times and construct a CI each time using this formula, roughly 95% of those intervals will contain the true μ. Any single interval either contains μ or it doesn&rsquo;t — the 95% is a coverage guarantee over hypothetical repetitions, not a probability about the specific interval.

## The traps that matter

**CLT needs finite variance.** Heavy-tailed distributions (Cauchy, Pareto with α &lt; 2) break the theorem. Sample means from such distributions do not converge to a Gaussian; they follow an α-stable law with fatter tails. Financial return data often falls in this regime; do not use naive CLT-based confidence intervals.

**iid is a strong assumption.** Correlated samples make the effective sample size smaller than N. A time series with autocorrelation of 0.9 might have effective N ≈ 0.1 N — meaning your CI is 3× too optimistic. Use block-bootstrap or autocorrelation-corrected variances.

**Small-N gives bad CI coverage.** For N &lt; 30, the sample standard deviation is a noisy estimate of σ; use Student&rsquo;s t distribution for the multiplier instead of 1.96. For N &lt; 10, be very careful — bootstrap or exact inference is more reliable.

**CLT is asymptotic — the constant matters.** &ldquo;CLT eventually kicks in&rdquo; is not a useful guarantee if your N is 20 and your data is skewed. Look at Q-Q plots of your sample means at your working N to check.

**Selection bias breaks LLN too.** LLN needs unbiased sampling. If you subselect samples in ways correlated with X (survivorship bias, non-response bias), the sample mean converges to a *different* number — the mean of the selected subpopulation, not the population.

**Log-transformed data may satisfy CLT better.** Multiplicative processes (product of many factors) produce log-normal distributions; the *log* of the data is Gaussian, and CLT applies more cleanly. Common trick in finance, biology, and hyperparameter search.

<RealWorldML title="SGD noise, A/B testing, and Monte Carlo — CLT in three places at once">

**SGD gradient noise.** A stochastic gradient estimate g_batch = (1/B) Σ ∇L(x_i) is a sample mean over the batch. By LLN, it converges to the true gradient ∇L̄ as B → ∞. By CLT, it&rsquo;s approximately 𝒩(∇L̄, Σ/B) for some covariance Σ that depends on the loss landscape. This Gaussian noise is *not a bug*: it acts as an implicit regularizer (SGD noise helps escape sharp minima) and is why small-batch training generalizes differently from large-batch. Modern "sharpness-aware minimization" and "SAM" methods explicitly exploit the CLT-shaped noise.

**A/B testing.** You&rsquo;re comparing conversion rates between two groups: 3.0% for control, 3.2% for treatment. Is the 0.2% lift real or noise? CLT: the sample means are approximately Gaussian with SE = √(p(1−p)/n) each; the difference is Gaussian with SE = √(SE_A² + SE_B²). Z-statistic = 0.002 / SE. If |Z| &gt; 1.96, you declare significance at 5%. Required sample size for detecting a lift of Δ at 80% power and 5% significance: n ≈ 16 · p(1 − p) / Δ² per arm. Every "how big should my A/B test be?" calculator is running this formula.

**Monte Carlo error bars.** You&rsquo;re estimating an RL policy&rsquo;s expected return by averaging returns over 100 rollouts. That average is a Monte Carlo estimator. Its error bar is SE = σ_rollout / √100. If σ_rollout is large (highly variable episodes), 100 rollouts gives you a wide CI. To halve the error, use 400 rollouts. To narrow the CI by 10×, use 10,000. This is *why* every RL benchmark you read reports "mean ± SE over N seeds/rollouts" — the CI shape is CLT-derived.

The pattern: every &ldquo;how much data do I need?&rdquo; and &ldquo;how confident am I in this estimate?&rdquo; question in ML has an answer with LLN (convergence guarantee) and CLT (rate + shape). Two theorems, endless applications.

</RealWorldML>

<Summary>

- **Law of large numbers**: for iid X_i with finite mean μ, the sample mean X̄_N → μ as N → ∞. Convergence is guaranteed — no rate.
- **Central limit theorem**: for iid X_i with finite mean μ and variance σ², the standardized sample mean Z_N = (X̄_N − μ) / (σ/√N) → 𝒩(0, 1). Rate is 1/√N.
- **1/√N law**: to reduce standard error by k, need k² more samples. Fundamental to sample-size planning.
- **95% CI for the mean**: X̄ ± 1.96 · s/√N for large N; Student&rsquo;s t for small N.
- **Requirements**: iid, finite variance. Heavy tails, strong dependence, or selection bias break the guarantees.
- **CLT is why every ML measurement has Gaussian-shaped error bars** — from test accuracy to A/B lifts to SGD gradient noise to Monte Carlo estimates.

</Summary>
