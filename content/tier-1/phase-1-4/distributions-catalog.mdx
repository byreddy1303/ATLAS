<Motivation title="Every ML modeling choice picks a distribution family — knowing the catalog is the difference between guessing and reasoning">

Distributions are the vocabulary of ML modeling. Every architectural or algorithmic choice implicitly picks one:

- **Classification loss = negative log-likelihood of a categorical.** Cross-entropy is literally −log p(true class) under the model&rsquo;s categorical distribution over classes. Every softmax layer is a parameterization of Categorical(π).
- **Regression loss = negative log-likelihood of a Gaussian.** MSE is (up to constants) −log p(y | ŷ) when y | ŷ ~ 𝒩(ŷ, σ²) for a fixed σ. Mean-absolute-error corresponds to a Laplace likelihood instead. Every loss function is a chosen distribution family.
- **VAE latents are Gaussians.** Encoder outputs μ(x), σ(x); the latent z ~ 𝒩(μ(x), diag(σ²(x))). KL to prior 𝒩(0, I) is closed-form because both are Gaussians.
- **Diffusion noise is standard Gaussian.** ε ~ 𝒩(0, I). Forward process is a scheduled sequence of Gaussians; reverse process learns to invert them.
- **Rewards in reinforcement learning are often modeled as exponential-family** (Gaussian, Gamma, Bernoulli depending on the domain). Policy gradients decompose neatly for exponential-family distributions.
- **Bayesian priors on positive quantities** (variances, precisions, rates) are usually Gamma or InverseGamma. Priors on probabilities are Beta or Dirichlet.

You don&rsquo;t need to memorize every distribution; you need to know the shapes and the typical settings so you can pattern-match. A count of arrivals per hour? Poisson. Time-until-event? Exponential. Probability of success? Beta prior. Ellipsoidal cluster? Multivariate Gaussian. This chapter builds that pattern library, then dives deep into the Gaussian (the most-important-single-distribution in ML).

</Motivation>

<Intuition title="A distribution family is a *shape* parameterized by a small set of knobs">

Each family is a parameterized shape. Turning a knob changes the shape, but the family stays what it is. **Gaussian** = a smooth bell, knobs = (mean, variance). **Bernoulli** = two vertical bars whose heights sum to 1, knob = P(success). **Poisson** = a decaying tail of bars over the non-negative integers, knob = rate. **Beta** = a curve on [0, 1] whose shape ranges from U-shaped to bell-shaped to skewed, knobs = (α, β).

Real data is never *exactly* any of these — but many phenomena are approximately one of them, and that&rsquo;s enough. Choosing a distribution is choosing which family&rsquo;s knobs to fit to the data. When the family fits well, downstream math becomes tractable (closed-form posteriors, low-variance estimators, calibrated confidence intervals). When it doesn&rsquo;t, you switch families or use a non-parametric method.

</Intuition>

<Recall term="PMF vs PDF">

Discrete distributions are described by a **PMF** p(x) = P(X = x). Continuous distributions by a **PDF** f(x); probability comes from integrating over intervals. Both are non-negative and total to 1 (sum or integral respectively). Named distributions specify a functional form and the parameters that appear in it.

</Recall>

## Discrete distributions

### Bernoulli(p)

One trial with a binary outcome. Support &#123;0, 1&#125;. Parameter p ∈ [0, 1] = P(X = 1).

$$
p(x) = p^x (1 - p)^{1-x} \quad \text{for } x \in \{0, 1\}, \qquad E[X] = p, \qquad \mathrm{Var}(X) = p(1 - p).
$$

Uses: binary labels, click-through rate, single coin flip. The build block of everything else discrete.

### Binomial(n, p)

Number of successes in n independent Bernoulli(p) trials. Support &#123;0, 1, …, n&#125;.

$$
p(k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1 - p)^{n-k}, \qquad E[X] = np, \qquad \mathrm{Var}(X) = np(1 - p).
$$

Uses: number of correct predictions on a test set of size n, batch-level success counts.

### Categorical / Multinoulli over K classes

Support = &#123;1, 2, …, K&#125;. Parameter π = (π₁, …, π_K) with π_k ≥ 0 and Σ π_k = 1.

$$
p(k) = \pi_k, \qquad E[\mathbf{1}\{X = k\}] = \pi_k.
$$

Uses: LLM next-token distribution (K = vocabulary size), image classification (K = number of classes), multi-class softmax output. The most-used distribution in modern ML by frequency.

### Poisson(λ)

Number of events in a fixed interval when events occur at average rate λ per interval, independently. Support = &#123;0, 1, 2, …&#125;.

$$
p(k) = \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!}, \qquad E[X] = \lambda, \qquad \mathrm{Var}(X) = \lambda.
$$

Uses: number of tokens generated before &lt;end&gt;, number of Bernoulli events aggregated over rare-event windows, count regression.

**Mean = variance** is the Poisson signature. If your count data has variance much larger than its mean, use Negative Binomial (a Poisson-Gamma mixture) instead — the phenomenon is called *overdispersion*.

## Continuous distributions (non-Gaussian)

### Uniform(a, b)

Equal density on [a, b], zero outside.

$$
f(x) = \frac{1}{b - a} \text{ for } x \in [a, b], \qquad E[X] = \frac{a+b}{2}, \qquad \mathrm{Var}(X) = \frac{(b-a)^2}{12}.
$$

Uses: default random-number generator output; noise in continuous latent spaces; base measure for inverse-CDF sampling.

### Exponential(λ)

Waiting time until the next event in a Poisson process of rate λ. Support [0, ∞).

$$
f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, \qquad F(x) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x}, \qquad E[X] = \frac{1}{\lambda}, \qquad \mathrm{Var}(X) = \frac{1}{\lambda^2}.
$$

**Memoryless property**: P(X &gt; s + t | X &gt; s) = P(X &gt; t). The only continuous distribution with this property. Uses: modeling times between events, decay processes, RL episode lengths under a constant termination rate.

### Beta(α, β)

Distribution on [0, 1]. Extremely flexible shape controlled by two positive parameters.

$$
f(x) = \frac{x^{\alpha-1}(1-x)^{\beta-1}}{B(\alpha, \beta)}, \qquad E[X] = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta}, \qquad \mathrm{Var}(X) = \frac{\alpha \beta}{(\alpha+\beta)^2 (\alpha+\beta+1)}.
$$

Shape zoo (make a mental picture):

- α = β = 1: Uniform(0, 1) (flat).
- α, β both &gt; 1: unimodal, peaked interior.
- α = β &lt; 1: U-shaped (mass at edges).
- α &gt; β: right-skewed toward 1. α &lt; β: left-skewed toward 0.

Uses: **conjugate prior for Bernoulli/Binomial** — start with Beta(α₀, β₀) prior, observe k successes in n trials, posterior is Beta(α₀ + k, β₀ + n − k). Also used for modeling proportions and probabilities of probabilities.

### Gamma(k, θ)

Distribution on [0, ∞). Two parameterizations exist — *shape-scale* (k, θ) and *shape-rate* (α, β = 1/θ). Shape k controls skewness; scale θ (or 1/rate) sets overall size.

$$
f(x) = \frac{x^{k-1} e^{-x/\theta}}{\Gamma(k) \theta^k}, \qquad E[X] = k \theta, \qquad \mathrm{Var}(X) = k \theta^2.
$$

**Special cases**: Gamma(1, θ) = Exponential(1/θ). Gamma(k, 2) = Chi-squared with 2k degrees of freedom. Sum of k iid Exp(1/θ) = Gamma(k, θ) — a common "waiting time until the k-th event" model.

Uses: **conjugate prior for the rate of a Poisson** or for the precision (1/σ²) of a Gaussian. Common in Bayesian modeling of positive quantities.

## The Gaussian (deep dive)

The Gaussian, or Normal, distribution 𝒩(μ, σ²) is the workhorse of applied probability. Every serious ML curriculum spends a large fraction of its time on it because it appears everywhere: the CLT (next chapter) makes it the natural distribution of averages and sums; the maximum-entropy distribution with fixed mean and variance is Gaussian; and its mathematical properties are uniquely convenient (linear combinations stay Gaussian, closed under conditioning, closed-form KL divergence).

$$
f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right), \qquad E[X] = \mu, \qquad \mathrm{Var}(X) = \sigma^2.
$$

**The 68–95–99.7 rule.** For X ~ 𝒩(μ, σ²):

- 68% of mass within ±σ of μ.
- 95% within ±2σ.
- 99.7% within ±3σ.

Beyond 4σ is astronomically rare (1 in ~16,000). This is why "the tails are thin" for Gaussians — extreme events are extraordinarily unlikely, which is *not* the case for heavier-tailed distributions like Cauchy or Pareto.

**Linear combinations stay Gaussian.** If X ~ 𝒩(μ_X, σ_X²) and Y ~ 𝒩(μ_Y, σ_Y²) are independent, then

$$
aX + bY \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}(a\mu_X + b\mu_Y,\; a^2 \sigma_X^2 + b^2 \sigma_Y^2).
$$

This linearity is what makes Gaussians tractable. Every step in a Kalman filter, every score matching argument in diffusion, every reparameterization trick in VAEs — all rely on this closure property.

**Standardization** transforms any Gaussian to the *standard* Gaussian 𝒩(0, 1): if X ~ 𝒩(μ, σ²), then Z = (X − μ)/σ ~ 𝒩(0, 1). Every practical Gaussian computation reduces to a lookup in tables (or scipy) of the standard normal CDF Φ.

## Multivariate Gaussian anatomy

A d-dimensional Gaussian 𝒩(μ, Σ) is parameterized by a mean vector μ ∈ ℝᵈ and a symmetric positive-definite covariance matrix Σ ∈ ℝᵈˣᵈ.

$$
f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2} |\Sigma|^{1/2}} \exp\left(-\tfrac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^\top \Sigma^{-1} (\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})\right).
$$

**Read the covariance matrix.**

- **Diagonal entries** Σ_ii = variance of the i-th coordinate.
- **Off-diagonal entries** Σ_ij = covariance of coordinates i and j (positive = they co-vary, negative = anti-correlated, zero = uncorrelated).
- **Eigenvectors of Σ = principal directions of the density ellipsoid.**
- **Square-roots of eigenvalues = half-lengths of the ellipsoid axes** in each principal direction.

**Marginalization is free**: any subset of coordinates is itself Gaussian, with mean and covariance found by simply dropping the other rows/columns of μ and Σ. **Conditioning is closed-form**: given X = (X_A, X_B) jointly Gaussian, the conditional X_A | X_B is Gaussian with mean and covariance computable via block-matrix inversion. This closure is exactly what makes Gaussian processes and Kalman filters tractable — you can propagate Gaussians through linear operations, marginalization, and conditioning without ever leaving the family.

## Choosing the right distribution — a cheat sheet

| Data | Distribution family |
|---|---|
| Binary label | Bernoulli |
| Count of successes out of n trials | Binomial |
| One of K categories (LLM next token) | Categorical |
| Count of rare events in a fixed interval | Poisson |
| Continuous real value with roughly-symmetric spread | Gaussian |
| Continuous positive value (times, sizes) | Exponential, Gamma, or Log-normal |
| Continuous value in [0, 1] (a probability) | Beta (or Dirichlet if multivariate) |
| Multivariate cluster with elliptical shape | Multivariate Gaussian |

Two rules of thumb before you commit:

- **Sanity-check the support.** A Gaussian model of ages will give negative ages some probability. Use a positive-supported distribution (log-normal, Gamma) if it&rsquo;s conceptually wrong for values to go negative.
- **Empirical histograms are your friend.** Plot the data. A tail that dies faster than Gaussian is *light-tailed* (bounded, sub-Gaussian). Fatter than Gaussian is *heavy-tailed*; consider Student-t, Cauchy, or Pareto for extreme tails.

## The traps that matter

**"Everything is Gaussian" is wrong.** The CLT says *averages* of iid variables tend to be Gaussian, not raw data. Real data often has heavy tails, skewness, or bounded support that Gaussians ignore. Fit a Gaussian to a heavy-tailed dataset and you&rsquo;ll systematically underestimate the tail probabilities — bad for risk management, safety-critical systems, and outlier detection.

**Mean and variance don&rsquo;t determine a distribution.** Two very different distributions (Gaussian, exponential, uniform on some range) can share a mean and variance. When someone reports "mean = 3, std = 1," you still know almost nothing about the shape. Ask for a histogram, or at minimum the quantiles.

**Poisson variance = mean is a strong assumption.** Real count data often has variance much larger than the mean (overdispersion). Test this before assuming Poisson; use Negative Binomial or a mixture if overdispersed.

**The Beta prior isn&rsquo;t neutral.** Beta(1, 1) = Uniform, sometimes called an "uninformative" prior, but it&rsquo;s not truly uninformative for all quantities. Jeffreys prior (Beta(0.5, 0.5)) is another choice. If your prior has strong weight relative to your data (α + β large), it will bias the posterior.

**Multivariate Gaussian requires PD covariance.** If Σ has zero or negative eigenvalues, the density is undefined (0 determinant → 1/0 in the formula). Numerically this manifests as `LinAlgError: not positive definite` when you try to compute log-likelihoods. Add a tiny diagonal jitter (Σ + εI) as a fix. This is exactly what Gaussian-process libraries do internally.

**Independence != uncorrelated for non-Gaussians.** For Gaussians, uncorrelated = independent. For non-Gaussians in general, uncorrelated is a weaker condition. Don&rsquo;t assume independence just because you saw zero correlation.

<RealWorldML title="Every ML architecture is a distribution choice">

Trace any modern GenAI system and you find a distribution at every interface:

- **Softmax over the vocabulary** in an LLM = Categorical(π) where π = softmax(logits). Temperature and top-p sharpen or restrict this measure. Cross-entropy loss during training is the negative log-likelihood of this Categorical.
- **VAE encoder output** = per-example Gaussian 𝒩(μ(x), diag(σ²(x))). The ELBO training objective is closed-form because KL between two diagonal Gaussians has a formula. Non-Gaussian variational families (normalizing-flow posteriors, discrete latents) are active research precisely because the Gaussian assumption is limiting.
- **Diffusion forward noise** = 𝒩(0, I). Reverse-process training loss is closed-form because the intermediate distributions are analytically Gaussians. Alternatives (categorical diffusion, Poisson diffusion) exist but are harder to train.
- **RLHF reward model** = often modeled as producing a Gaussian scalar per completion. Policy update depends on the *distribution* of rewards, not just a point estimate. Assumptions about reward variance affect KL constraints in PPO.
- **Gaussian processes for hyperparameter tuning** (Bayesian optimization) = fully Gaussian belief over the loss landscape. Every acquisition function (EI, UCB, TS) is an integral against a Gaussian posterior.

The list goes on. Every time you see a `torch.distributions.Normal` or a softmax or a `sample()` call in ML code, you&rsquo;re reaching for one of these named families. Knowing which one to reach for — and being able to argue *why* — is the mark of an ML engineer who has this chapter internalized.

</RealWorldML>

<CodeItYourself title="Three drills — fitting, sampling, likelihood">

1. **Fit each family to random data.** Generate 5000 samples from a mystery mixture (e.g., 70% 𝒩(0, 1) + 30% 𝒩(4, 0.5²)). Fit a single Gaussian, a single exponential, and a single Beta (after rescaling to [0, 1]) via `scipy.stats.<dist>.fit`. Compare log-likelihoods. Note: the single-Gaussian fit will be much worse than a two-component mixture would be — this is the everyday reality of "real data isn&rsquo;t any one distribution."

2. **Beta prior updating.** You&rsquo;re modeling P(click) for a new ad. Prior: Beta(2, 8) (biased toward low CTR since most ads underperform). Observe 30 clicks in 200 impressions. Compute the posterior Beta(2 + 30, 8 + 200 − 30) = Beta(32, 178). Plot prior vs posterior — the posterior should be sharply peaked around 30/200 = 0.15. Try shrinking the prior to Beta(0.5, 0.5) and see how much wider the posterior gets.

3. **Multivariate Gaussian conditional.** Generate 1000 samples from a 2D Gaussian with a positively-correlated covariance. Condition on X₁ = 1.0 (approximately — restrict to |X₁ − 1| &lt; 0.05). The conditional distribution of X₂ | X₁ = 1 should itself be Gaussian, with mean and variance predicted by the closed-form conditioning formula for jointly Gaussian variables. Verify by histogram.

</CodeItYourself>

<Summary>

- **Discrete**: Bernoulli (single trial), Binomial (n trials), Categorical (K outcomes, LLM softmax), Poisson (rare event counts, mean = variance).
- **Continuous**: Uniform (equal density), Exponential (memoryless waiting time), Beta (on [0, 1], conjugate prior for Bernoulli), Gamma (on [0, ∞), conjugate prior for Poisson rate and Gaussian precision).
- **Gaussian 𝒩(μ, σ²)**: bell shape, 68–95–99.7 rule, linear combinations stay Gaussian, standardization Z = (X − μ)/σ.
- **Multivariate Gaussian 𝒩(μ, Σ)**: Σ must be symmetric PD. Eigenvectors of Σ = principal axes; √eigenvalues = axis half-lengths. Marginalization by dropping rows/columns; conditioning is closed-form.
- **Match distribution to data support and shape**: positive → Exp/Gamma, [0, 1] → Beta, count → Poisson, real-valued → Gaussian (or heavier-tailed if tails matter).
- **Every ML loss is a likelihood**: MSE ↔ Gaussian, cross-entropy ↔ Categorical, MAE ↔ Laplace, Poisson-NLL ↔ Poisson.

</Summary>
