# As rho -> infinity, the penalty forces the constraint to be satisfied.
import numpy as np

def penalized(w, rho):
    x, y = w
    return x**2 + y**2 + rho * (x + y - 1) ** 2

def dpenalized(w, rho):
    x, y = w
    dx = 2*x + 2 * rho * (x + y - 1)
    dy = 2*y + 2 * rho * (x + y - 1)
    return np.array([dx, dy])

def gd(rho, x0=np.array([2.0, 2.0]), lr=0.1, steps=200):
    w = x0.copy()
    for _ in range(steps):
        w = w - lr * dpenalized(w, rho)
    return w

for rho in [0.1, 1, 10, 100, 1000]:

# The KL penalty is the Lagrangian relaxation of the constraint KL(policy || ref) <= budget.
import numpy as np

# Toy setup: 4 possible actions; reward vector R; reference policy p_ref (softmax over some logits)

<Motivation title="Half of modern ML tricks are constrained optimization in disguise">

You've seen **unconstrained optimization**: pick a loss L(θ), take gradients, minimize. Nothing standing in the way. But look at what modern GenAI systems actually do:

- **RLHF** maximizes reward *subject to* a KL constraint: "keep the fine-tuned policy close to the reference." The KL is a **hard constraint** with a budget.
- **PPO** (Proximal Policy Optimization) clips gradient updates so the new policy stays inside a "trust region" around the old one — an **inequality constraint** on how far you can move per step.
- **SVMs** minimize the weight vector's size *subject to* the classifier margin being ≥ 1 on every training point.
- **Weight decay** in Adam is a **soft constraint** on how big the parameters can grow — implemented as an L2 penalty, but the underlying problem is really "minimize training loss subject to ‖w‖ being bounded."

Quick vocabulary before we dig in:

- **Constraint** = a rule the solution must obey (like "the KL divergence can't exceed 5" or "the norm of weights ≤ 10").
- **Lagrangian** = a clever trick that turns constrained problems into unconstrained ones, by adding a "penalty term" for violating the constraint. You'll see this word everywhere.
- **KKT conditions** (Karush-Kuhn-Tucker — three people's names) = the algebraic equations you solve to find optimal constrained solutions.
- **Duality** = a beautiful theory that relates the original ("primal") problem to a mirror-image ("dual") one, often easier to solve.

Understanding constrained optimization unlocks two things for a GenAI engineer:

1. **Reading modern papers.** When a paper says "we optimize L subject to some KL bound" and another says "we add a KL penalty with coefficient β," those two formulations are related by Lagrangian duality. Once you know the connection, you understand what β is doing and how to pick it.
2. **Building trust-region-style updates.** If you want to fine-tune a model without destroying its prior behavior, you need to know how to write a distance constraint on parameters or on output distributions.

This chapter builds the machinery: **Lagrangian**, **KKT conditions**, **duality**, and the two ways to enforce constraints in practice — hard (Lagrangian) and soft (penalty).

</Motivation>

<Intuition title="Lagrangian = objective + shadow price × constraint violation">

Imagine you're minimizing a loss f(x) but a constraint g(x) ≤ 0 must be satisfied. The **Lagrangian** is:

$$
L(x, \lambda) = f(x) + \lambda \cdot g(x), \quad \lambda \ge 0
$$

The multiplier λ is a **shadow price**: for each unit of constraint violation, we pay λ · violation in the objective. The optimal λ is exactly the "market price" of relaxing the constraint by one unit — i.e., how much the objective would improve if you were allowed to violate the constraint slightly.

**Two extreme cases:**

- **Constraint not active** at the optimum (`g(x*) < 0`): the constraint is slack. The shadow price is zero — you don't need to pay anything to satisfy something that's already true. So λ* = 0.
- **Constraint active** at the optimum (`g(x*) = 0`): the constraint is tight; the optimum sits ON the constraint boundary. The shadow price is positive — you'd love to loosen it. So λ* > 0.

**The KKT complementary-slackness condition** encodes both cases: `λ* · g(x*) = 0`. Either λ* = 0 (constraint slack) OR g(x*) = 0 (constraint active). Never both non-zero.

**Solving the Lagrangian:** find x* and λ* such that ∇_x L(x*, λ*) = 0 AND the constraint is satisfied. For convex problems, this is enough. For nonconvex, it's necessary but not sufficient.

</Intuition>

<Recall term="Feasible region">

The **feasible region** of a constrained problem is the set of all points that satisfy every constraint. Optimization is: find the minimum of the objective **inside the feasible region**. If the unconstrained minimum happens to lie in the feasible region, the constrained problem is trivial. When it doesn't, the constrained optimum sits **on the boundary** of the feasible region — and that's the case the Lagrangian and KKT machinery is built for.

</Recall>

## Equality-constrained problems

**The problem.** Minimize f(x) subject to g(x) = 0 (one or more equality constraints).

**The Lagrangian.** Introduce a multiplier λ for each constraint:

$$
L(x, \lambda) = f(x) - \lambda \cdot g(x)
$$

(Sign convention: we subtract λ·g here; other books add. Either works — the sign of λ just flips.)

**The first-order conditions.** At an optimum, the gradient of L with respect to both x and λ must be zero:

$$
\nabla_x L = \nabla f(x) - \lambda \nabla g(x) = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \nabla f(x^*) = \lambda^* \nabla g(x^*)
$$
$$
\nabla_\lambda L = -g(x) = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad g(x^*) = 0
$$

**Geometric picture.** At the constrained optimum, `∇f` is parallel to `∇g` — the objective's contour lines are **tangent** to the constraint surface. If ∇f had any component along the constraint surface, you could move slightly to reduce f while staying feasible.

## Inequality constraints and KKT

**The problem.** Minimize f(x) subject to g_i(x) ≤ 0 for i = 1..m (inequality constraints) and h_j(x) = 0 for j = 1..p (equality constraints).

**The Lagrangian.**

$$
L(x, \lambda, \nu) = f(x) + \sum_i \lambda_i g_i(x) + \sum_j \nu_j h_j(x)
$$

with `λ_i ≥ 0` (inequality multipliers must be nonneg — a slack constraint gets zero price, an active one gets positive) and `ν_j` free.

**The KKT conditions** (necessary at any local optimum, sufficient under convex problem + Slater):

1. **Stationarity:** `∇_x L = 0`
2. **Primal feasibility:** `g_i(x*) ≤ 0`, `h_j(x*) = 0`
3. **Dual feasibility:** `λ_i* ≥ 0`
4. **Complementary slackness:** `λ_i* · g_i(x*) = 0` for each `i`

That fourth condition is the key: for each inequality constraint, either the constraint is active (`g_i = 0`) or its multiplier is zero (`λ_i = 0`).

**Example: SVM classifier.** Minimize ½‖w‖² subject to `y_i (w·x_i + b) ≥ 1` for each training point. KKT gives: only training points **on the margin boundary** (`y_i(w·x_i + b) = 1`) have non-zero multipliers. Those are the "support vectors." Everything else has α_i = 0 and can be forgotten. That's the origin of the name.

## The dual problem

For each choice of λ ≥ 0 and ν, the **Lagrange dual function** is:

$$
q(\lambda, \nu) = \min_x L(x, \lambda, \nu)
$$

Two key facts:

- `q(λ, ν)` is **always concave** in `(λ, ν)`, regardless of whether `f` is convex — because it's an infimum of linear functions.
- `q(λ, ν) ≤ f(x*)` for every feasible λ, ν. This is **weak duality** — the dual gives a **lower bound** on the primal optimum.

**Strong duality:** for convex `f` and constraints satisfying Slater's condition (there exists a strictly-feasible point), we have `max_{λ,ν} q(λ, ν) = f(x*)`. Solving the dual gives the primal answer.

**Why we care about the dual:**

- **It's often easier.** SVM's primal has n weights + n margin constraints; the dual has n Lagrange multipliers with a simpler structure and works entirely with inner-products `<x_i, x_j>` — enabling the **kernel trick**.
- **Certificate of optimality.** Reach `primal = dual` and you've proven you're at the optimum. LP solvers use this for correctness.
- **Sensitivity analysis.** The optimal λ_i is the shadow price of constraint i — how much the objective would improve if you relaxed constraint i by one unit.

## Soft constraints — the penalty method

**Hard constraints** (as above) enforce `g(x) ≤ 0` exactly. **Soft constraints** are enforced approximately by adding a penalty term:

$$
\text{minimize} \quad f(x) + \rho \cdot g(x)^2
$$

for some penalty weight `ρ > 0`. As `ρ → ∞`, the penalized problem's optimum approaches the constrained optimum.

<RunnableCode title="Penalty method — sweep ρ to see the solution converge to the constrained optimum" code={penaltyMethodCode} packages={["numpy"]} />

**Trade-off.** Small `ρ` = the constraint is barely enforced (solution is close to unconstrained). Large `ρ` = the constraint is nearly enforced but the loss becomes **ill-conditioned** (Hessian eigenvalue ratio scales as `ρ`), which makes gradient descent slow. Modern practice: use a moderate `ρ`, accept some constraint slack, and treat the penalty weight as a hyperparameter.

**Connection to Lagrangian:** the penalty method with an increasing schedule of ρ is called the **exterior penalty method**. Set ρ correctly and it converges to the same solution as the Lagrangian. In practice, augmented Lagrangian methods combine both ideas (add both a Lagrange multiplier term and a penalty term).

<RealWorldML title="RLHF's KL constraint is Lagrangian duality">

The standard RLHF training loss is:

$$
\mathcal{L}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_\theta}[R(y)] + \beta \cdot \mathrm{KL}(\pi_\theta \parallel \pi_{\mathrm{ref}})
$$

Where π_θ is the fine-tuned policy, π_ref is the (frozen) reference policy, R is the reward model, and β is a hyperparameter.

**This is exactly the Lagrangian relaxation of a constrained problem:**

$$
\text{maximize} \quad \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_\theta}[R(y)] \quad \text{subject to} \quad \mathrm{KL}(\pi_\theta \parallel \pi_{\mathrm{ref}}) \le B
$$

for some budget `B`, with Lagrangian:

$$
L(\theta, \lambda) = -\mathbb{E}[R] + \lambda \cdot (\mathrm{KL} - B)
$$

The `β` in the RLHF loss IS the Lagrange multiplier `λ*` — the shadow price of the KL constraint. Different papers pick β differently: some fix β to a constant (soft constraint), others adapt β to keep KL close to a target budget (essentially solving the dual).

**In closed form** (for a bandit setup — single-step RL), the reward-maximizing policy under KL penalty is:

$$
\pi^*(y) \propto \pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y) \cdot \exp(R(y) / \beta)
$$

This is exactly the "reward-weighted policy" that shows up in DPO's derivation. β = 0 makes the policy delta-function on the reward-maximizing y. β → ∞ makes the policy equal to π_ref (no reward chasing).

**PPO's clipping** is a soft-constraint alternative: enforce that the policy ratio π_θ(y) / π_old(y) stays within [1 − ε, 1 + ε]. Instead of a Lagrangian, PPO uses a min-max clip. It's less clean mathematically but empirically robust to hyperparameter choice.

</RealWorldML>

<Summary>

- **Constrained optimization** = minimize f(x) subject to g(x) ≤ 0 or g(x) = 0.
- **Lagrangian:** L(x, λ) = f(x) + λ·g(x). Set gradients to zero AND constraints satisfied.
- **KKT conditions:** stationarity, primal feasibility, dual feasibility (λ ≥ 0), complementary slackness (λ·g = 0). Necessary at any local optimum; sufficient for convex problems under Slater's.
- **Duality:** q(λ) = min_x L(x, λ) is always concave. Weak duality: q ≤ primal. Strong duality: q_max = primal_min (holds for convex + Slater).
- **Soft constraints:** add ρ · g(x)² to the loss. As ρ → ∞, approaches the hard constraint but Hessian gets ill-conditioned.
- **RLHF loss = Lagrangian relaxation** of "max reward subject to KL ≤ budget." The β coefficient is the KL constraint's shadow price.
- **Closed-form RLHF policy:** π* ∝ π_ref · exp(R/β) — the reward-weighted policy. Underpins DPO and every RLHF derivation.

</Summary>
