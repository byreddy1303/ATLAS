<Motivation title="Convex is 'solved'; non-convex is 'we shipped it anyway'">

Every classical-ML textbook opens with convex problems (linear regression, logistic regression, SVMs) and promises: **gradient descent will find THE minimum, no matter where you start.** Every modern deep-learning result operates in the opposite universe — non-convex, chaotic, no such guarantees — and shipped anyway.

For a GenAI engineer, understanding convexity is a bright-line test that separates two very different worlds:

- On the **convex** side, math promises success. Any local minimum is the global one. Convergence rates are provable. The choice of starting point doesn't matter. If your loss is convex, you can just fit it.
- On the **non-convex** side (every neural net you'll ever train), math promises nothing. Different starting points reach different minima. GD can get stuck. Any convergence result is empirical, not proven.

Both worlds coexist in real ML systems. Your logistic regression baseline is convex. The regularizer you add to a neural-net loss (L2 weight decay) is convex. Your neural net's loss is not. Knowing which parts of your system are convex tells you where you can trust the math, and where you have to fall back on empirical observation. It also tells you when adding a convex regularizer stabilizes an otherwise-hopeless non-convex problem.

This chapter covers three questions:

1. What does "convex" actually mean, in three equivalent forms?
2. Why does convexity give you global optimality?
3. When your loss is non-convex (i.e., any deep learning), what changes — and what does modern practice do about it?

</Motivation>

<Intuition title="Convex is a bowl; non-convex is a mountain range">

**A convex function looks like a bowl.** No matter where you stand inside the bowl, gravity pulls you toward the same bottom. Roll a marble in, and it reaches the unique lowest point — regardless of where you dropped it in from. All the popular classical losses (squared error, logistic, hinge) draw bowls of one shape or another.

**A non-convex function looks like a mountain range.** Valleys, ridges, peaks, saddles. Where you land depends on where you started. Roll a marble in from the left side and it settles in the western valley; roll one from the right and it settles in the eastern one — and those two valleys can be at different heights. Deep-learning losses are like this, except in millions of dimensions, so the analogy breaks down long before you finish drawing it.

Formally, a function is convex iff any straight line drawn between two points on its graph lies **above** the graph. If any such line dips below, the function has a "dip" between the two points that a straight line can't reach — that's non-convex. This "chord test" is definition #1 below.

</Intuition>

<Recall term="Local minimum vs global minimum">

A **local minimum** is a point where the function is smaller than at every nearby point. A **global minimum** is the very smallest value the function takes anywhere. Every global minimum is also a local one. The reverse is false in general: a function can have many local minima, only one of which is the global one.

For **convex** functions, this distinction collapses — every local minimum is global. That single fact is why convexity is so prized: any minimum you find is THE minimum.

</Recall>

## Three equivalent definitions of "convex"

A function `f: R^n → R` is **convex** iff any of the following (equivalent) conditions hold:

**1. Chord test (geometric).** For any two points x, y in the domain and any t in [0, 1]:

$$
f(t x + (1-t) y) \le t\, f(x) + (1-t)\, f(y)
$$

Plain English: the value at the interpolated point is at most the linearly-interpolated value. The graph never rises above the chord between two of its points.

**2. Second-order (calculus).** If `f` is twice differentiable, then `f` is convex iff its Hessian is **positive semi-definite (PSD)** everywhere:

$$
\nabla^2 f(x) \succeq 0 \quad \text{for all } x
$$

In 1-D this reduces to `f''(x) ≥ 0` — the function is curving up (or flat). In n-D you need the Hessian's eigenvalues all `≥ 0` at every point. If they're strictly `> 0` (positive definite), `f` is **strictly convex** — there's a unique minimum, no plateau.

**3. First-order (linear-lower-bound).** If `f` is differentiable:

$$
f(y) \ge f(x) + \nabla f(x) \cdot (y - x) \quad \text{for all } x, y
$$

Plain English: at every point x, the tangent hyperplane lies below the graph. Every convex function is bounded from below by any of its tangents. This is the identity that powers most convex-optimization proofs (including GD's convergence rate).

**Pitfall — non-differentiable at a point is not the same as non-convex.** `f(x) = |x|` has a corner at 0, so it's not differentiable there, but it's convex (the chord test holds). Convex analysis was built to handle exactly this case — replace "gradient" with **subgradient** (any slope that stays below the graph) and everything works.

## Why convex → local minimum = global minimum

Suppose `f` is convex and `x*` is a **local** minimum. We want to prove `f(x*) ≤ f(y)` for **every** `y`.

**Step 1.** Local minimality means: there exists ε > 0 such that `f(x*) ≤ f(z)` for all `z` with `||z − x*|| < ε`.

**Step 2.** Take any other point y in the domain. Consider the segment from x* to y. For small enough t > 0, the point `z = t·y + (1 − t)·x*` sits within distance ε of x*, so by step 1:

$$
f(x^*) \le f(z) = f(t\, y + (1 - t)\, x^*)
$$

**Step 3.** Apply the chord test (definition 1):

$$
f(z) = f(t\, y + (1 - t)\, x^*) \le t\, f(y) + (1 - t)\, f(x^*)
$$

**Step 4.** Combining steps 2 and 3:

$$
f(x^*) \le t\, f(y) + (1 - t)\, f(x^*)
$$

Subtract `(1 - t) f(x*)` from both sides:

$$
t\, f(x^*) \le t\, f(y) \implies f(x^*) \le f(y) \quad \blacksquare
$$

Because y was arbitrary, `x*` is a global minimum. **This is the fundamental theorem of convex optimization.** It's why classical ML sleeps well at night: convex loss + reach any minimum ⇒ done.

## Convex is "solved" — GD converges at O(1/k)

For a convex, differentiable `f` with Lipschitz gradient (`||∇f(x) − ∇f(y)|| ≤ L ||x − y||`), gradient descent with step size `η = 1/L` satisfies:

$$
f(x_k) - f(x^*) \le \frac{L\, \|x_0 - x^*\|^2}{2 k}
$$

In plain English: the loss after k steps is at most `1/k` above the global minimum. For **strongly** convex `f` (Hessian ≥ μI for some μ > 0), this improves to a **geometric** rate:

$$
f(x_k) - f(x^*) \le (1 - \mu/L)^k \cdot \text{(initial gap)}
$$

so error decays exponentially — you get roughly `log(1/ε) / log(1/(1-μ/L))` iterations for error ε. These aren't heuristics; they're theorems.

Modern convex optimization also has algorithms that beat plain GD: **Nesterov's accelerated gradient** achieves `O(1/k²)`; **interior-point methods** for linear programs solve them to any precision in polynomial time. There's a whole ecosystem (CVXPY, MOSEK, Gurobi) that will solve "any" convex problem for you if you write it in the right form.

## Neural networks are not convex

Every layer of a neural net contains a **product** of parameter matrices (or a nonlinearity applied to one). Products of parameters are inherently non-convex in those parameters:

- `f(w_1, w_2) = (w_1 · w_2 − y)^2` has multiple minima because `(w_1, w_2)` and `(-w_1, -w_2)` give the same product.
- With ReLU nonlinearities in between, the landscape gets even worse: piecewise-quadratic patches meeting at "kinks" that aren't smooth.

**The two headline non-convex phenomena:**

- **Saddle points dominate.** In high dimensions, most stationary points (points where the gradient is zero) are **saddles**, not local minima. Some directions curve up, others curve down. Vanilla GD near a saddle takes tiny steps because the gradient is small — one reason why momentum, Adam, and second-order methods matter for non-convex training.
- **Bad local minima are rare (empirically).** Extensive study of neural-net loss landscapes (Choromanska et al. 2015, Kawaguchi 2016) suggests that most local minima of over-parameterized nets are close in loss value to the global minimum. This is not proven for general architectures, but is the working assumption behind why SGD-based training reliably reaches good solutions.

<RealWorldML title="Why we still train non-convex neural nets, and it works">

Every modern LLM training run — GPT-4, Claude, Llama, Gemini — is non-convex, has no known convergence guarantees, and yet works. Five empirical facts explain how:

**1. Over-parameterization.** Deep networks have far more parameters than training data points. The non-convex loss landscape has an enormous **manifold** of near-zero-loss solutions rather than isolated minima. Reaching one is much easier than reaching a specific one.

**2. Mode connectivity.** Different SGD solutions turn out to be connected by low-loss paths (Garipov et al. 2018). What look like "different local minima" from the outside are often points on the same low-loss basin, just seen from different SGD trajectories.

**3. Saddle points, not sharp local minima, are the sticking points.** Momentum and Adam's per-parameter adaptive step size specifically help escape saddles — one reason plain GD is rarely used for deep nets.

**4. Convex sub-problems inside the non-convex whole.** The last layer of a neural net (a linear classifier on frozen features) is **convex** in its weights, given the features. Fine-tuning only the head is convex-optimization-easy. LoRA effectively adds a small-rank convex perturbation to a frozen base.

**5. Regularization is convex.** L2 weight decay adds `λ ||w||²` to any loss. That regularizer is convex. Even in a non-convex problem, the regularizer smooths the landscape and biases the solution toward low-norm regions — a stability boost. RLHF's KL penalty is similarly a convex "pull" toward the reference policy.

<Summary>

- **Convex** = the graph lies below every chord between its points; equivalently, Hessian is PSD everywhere (if twice differentiable).
- **Convex ⇒ every local minimum is global.** Proof is 4 lines: local minimality + chord test.
- **Convex optimization is "solved":** GD converges at O(1/k) with a fixed step; strong convexity gives geometric rate. Interior-point methods and CVXPY-family solvers handle "any" convex problem.
- **Deep learning is non-convex** because parameter products (and ReLU kinks) break convexity. GD's convergence guarantees don't apply.
- **In practice, non-convex training works** because of over-parameterization (many near-optimal solutions), mode connectivity, adaptive optimizers that escape saddles, convex sub-problems (last layer, LoRA head), and convex regularizers.
- **Litmus test for your ML system:** which parts are convex? Those parts have theory. The rest ships on empirical observation.

</Summary>
