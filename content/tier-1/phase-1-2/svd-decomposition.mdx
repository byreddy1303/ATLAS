export const svdBasicCode = `import numpy as np

# rectangular matrix — no eigen-decomposition possible, but SVD always works
A = np.array([[3.0,  1.0],
              [1.0,  3.0],
              [1.0,  1.0]])                             # 3x2

U, s, Vt = np.linalg.svd(A, full_matrices=False)       # thin SVD

print("A shape        =", A.shape)
print("U shape        =", U.shape, "  (columns: orthonormal 'output' basis)")
print("singular values=", np.round(s, 4))
print("V^T shape      =", Vt.shape, "  (rows: orthonormal 'input' basis)")

# reconstruct: A = U diag(s) V^T
Sigma = np.diag(s)
A_recon = U @ Sigma @ Vt
print()
print("U @ diag(s) @ V^T =")
print(np.round(A_recon, 6))
print("A =")
print(A)

# U and V^T are orthonormal — verify
print()
print("U^T U =",  np.round(U.T @ U, 6))                 # 2x2 identity
print("V V^T =",  np.round(Vt @ Vt.T, 6))               # 2x2 identity`;

<Motivation title="The single decomposition every GenAI engineer needs to know">

**SVD** (Singular Value Decomposition) is the "master decomposition" of linear algebra. It says: **every matrix can be broken into three pieces — a rotation, a stretch, and another rotation.** That single fact powers a huge chunk of modern deep learning.

Two of the biggest applications you'll hit:

- **LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation)** — the standard trick for parameter-efficient fine-tuning of large language models. It represents each weight update as a product of two thin rectangles: ΔW = B·A. That's a rank-r decomposition. SVD is the math machine that says "any matrix has a best rank-r approximation, and here it is."
- **Model compression, embedding compression, activation caching** — all reduce to "approximate this matrix with a simpler one." Truncated SVD gives the *provably best* approximation of a given rank (a result called the **Eckart–Young theorem**).

Quick vocabulary so nothing sounds scary:

- **Rank** = "the number of independent directions in a matrix." Full-rank means every direction matters; low-rank means most of the matrix can be described by a few directions.
- **Singular value** = the amount of "stretch" along each direction. Big singular values matter a lot; small ones can be safely ignored.
- **Rotation** = "spin things around without stretching." Formally, an **orthogonal matrix**.

SVD also unlocks the analyses you'll need to understand a model's guts: the singular values of the attention pattern tell you whether a head is focused or diffuse; the singular values of the weight matrix tell you the effective dimensionality of the map (a k=64 head with rank 4 is wasting capacity); the singular values of the covariance matrix are the variances of the principal components in PCA (via `SVD(X) → Σ² = variances`, no covariance-matrix construction needed — a numerical win).

More broadly: **SVD works for any matrix**, not just square symmetric ones — rectangular, non-square, complex-eigenvalue, defective, whatever you throw at it. Eigen-decomposition has caveats; SVD has none. That's the deep reason it's the workhorse decomposition of numerical linear algebra.

The three-part deliverable of this chapter: (1) know the geometric picture of SVD, so you can *see* what a weight matrix does; (2) know the algebraic form A = UΣVᵀ, so you can read code; (3) know Eckart–Young + truncation, so you can reason about compression and rank.

</Motivation>

<Intuition title="Rotate, stretch, rotate — any matrix, exactly">

Every m×n matrix A can be written as a product of three specific pieces:

$$
A \;=\; U\,\Sigma\,V^{\mathsf T}
$$

where:

- **V** is an n×n **orthogonal** matrix — its columns are an orthonormal basis for the input space ℝⁿ (the "right singular vectors"). Vᵀ acts as a rotation of the input.
- **Σ** is an m×n matrix with non-negative entries on the diagonal (the **singular values** σ₁ ≥ σ₂ ≥ … ≥ 0) and zeros elsewhere. It scales each new axis independently.
- **U** is an m×m **orthogonal** matrix — its columns are an orthonormal basis for the output space ℝᵐ (the "left singular vectors"). U rotates the stretched result into the output space.

Geometrically: to apply A to a vector x, **first rotate x** (via Vᵀ), then **stretch along each new axis** by the corresponding σ_i (via Σ), then **rotate the result** (via U) into the output space. Every matrix, no matter how ugly, is exactly one of these three-step maps — once you find the right pair of orthogonal frames.

Concretely: a unit circle in the input space is rotated (still a circle), stretched into an ellipse with semi-axes equal to the σ_i (aligned with the coordinate axes because Σ is diagonal), then rotated to a rotated ellipse in the output space. **The singular values are the lengths of the semi-axes of that ellipse.** That's what σ_i *is* — a stretch factor along a specific right singular direction.

</Intuition>

<Recall term="Orthogonal matrix">

An orthogonal matrix Q satisfies QᵀQ = I, equivalently Q⁻¹ = Qᵀ — its columns (and rows) are orthonormal. Applying Q rotates (and possibly reflects) space without distorting lengths or angles. Rotations and reflections are the two ways a matrix can be "rigid"; both are captured by orthogonality.

</Recall>

## The SVD in practice — every matrix, guaranteed

For an m×n matrix A of any shape:

$$
A \;=\; U_{m \times m}\; \Sigma_{m \times n}\; V^{\mathsf T}_{n \times n}
$$

with U, V orthogonal, Σ non-negative diagonal (with r = rank(A) positive entries). The decomposition exists for every matrix — this is the payoff of SVD over eigendecomposition:

- **Non-square matrices** — SVD works; eigen doesn't (eigenvalues only exist for square matrices).
- **Defective matrices** (repeated eigenvalues with too few eigenvectors) — SVD works; diagonalization fails.
- **Complex eigenvalues** for real matrices — SVD still returns real orthogonal U, V and real non-negative Σ.

In code, `np.linalg.svd(A)` returns `U, s, Vt` where `s` is the 1-D array of singular values (already sorted descending) and `Vt` is Vᵀ (already transposed for convenience).

## The geometric picture — the animation you should carry in your head

Watch a unit circle in ℝ² go through the three stages of an SVD:

<RunnableCode title="The unit circle -> ellipse animation, made explicit" code={geometryCode} packages={["numpy", "matplotlib"]} captureFig={true} />

Read the plots left-to-right:

1. **Start**: unit circle in the input space.
2. **After Vᵀ**: a rotated circle — Vᵀ preserves lengths and angles, so it's still a circle of the same radius, just re-aligned so that the "important input directions" (right singular vectors) coincide with the standard axes.
3. **After Σ**: an ellipse aligned with the standard axes. The semi-axis in the x-direction has length σ₁; the y-semi-axis has length σ₂. Each direction's independent stretch.
4. **After U**: a rotated ellipse in the output space — same shape as (3), rotated by U into whatever orientation A actually produces. This is the image of the unit circle under A: an ellipse whose axes are the columns of U (up to length) and whose semi-axes are the σ_i.

Every 2×2 matrix's "action on space" is a rotated ellipse. Every 3×3 matrix's action is a rotated ellipsoid. In n dimensions it's the same picture with n singular values telling you how much stretching happens in each of n orthogonal directions.

## Where SVD comes from — an eigen-problem in disguise

SVD isn't a fundamentally new object — it's the eigen-decomposition of a *symmetric* matrix built from A, translated back. Compute `AᵀA`:

- **AᵀA is symmetric** (obvious: (AᵀA)ᵀ = AᵀA).
- **AᵀA is positive semi-definite** (all eigenvalues ≥ 0), because for any vector x, xᵀ(AᵀA)x = ‖Ax‖² ≥ 0.

So AᵀA has real, non-negative eigenvalues and an orthonormal eigenbasis (by the spectral theorem). It turns out:

- The **right singular vectors of A** (columns of V) are the eigenvectors of AᵀA.
- The **singular values of A** are the square roots of the eigenvalues of AᵀA: σ_i = √λ_i.
- The **left singular vectors of A** (columns of U) can be computed as `u_i = Av_i / σ_i` (or as eigenvectors of `AAᵀ`, which has the same nonzero eigenvalues as AᵀA).

<RunnableCode title="SVD via eigen-decomposition of A^T A" code={relationEigenCode} packages={["numpy"]} />

That's the theoretical connection. In practice, computing SVD via `AᵀA` is *numerically bad* — squaring A doubles the condition number and drops precision. Real libraries use dedicated SVD algorithms (Golub–Reinsch, Jacobi rotations) that work on A directly. But the connection to eigen is why SVD "just works" for any matrix — every A gives rise to a symmetric AᵀA, and the spectral theorem handles it.

## Full, thin, and truncated SVD

Three flavors of the same decomposition:

- **Full SVD**: U is m×m, Σ is m×n, Vᵀ is n×n. Costs the full O(mn·min(m,n)) but gives you complete orthonormal bases for both spaces (including the nullspace and left-nullspace, via the "extra" columns of V and U).
- **Thin (reduced) SVD**: U is m×r, Σ is r×r, Vᵀ is r×n, where r = min(m, n). Drops the extra orthogonal columns that get multiplied by zero singular values anyway. Same product A = UΣVᵀ, less storage. NumPy's `full_matrices=False` gives you this — usually what you want.
- **Truncated (rank-k) SVD**: keep only the top k singular values and their vectors. `A_k = U[:, :k] @ diag(s[:k]) @ Vt[:k]`. This is *not* an exact factorization — it's an approximation.

The truncated form is the star. It's a way to compress a matrix by keeping only the most significant rank-1 pieces. By the outer-product view of matmul, `U Σ Vᵀ = Σ_i σ_i u_i outer v_i` — a sum of rank-1 outer products, weighted by σ_i. **Truncated SVD keeps the biggest few**:

$$
A_k \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sigma_i\, \mathbf{u}_i\, \mathbf{v}_i^{\mathsf T}
$$

Storage: k(m + n + 1) numbers vs the original mn. When k ≪ min(m, n), that's a huge compression — and by Eckart–Young below, it's the *best possible* rank-k approximation.

## Eckart–Young — truncation is optimal

The **Eckart–Young theorem** states that among all rank-≤k matrices, the truncated SVD `A_k` minimizes the reconstruction error in both the Frobenius norm and the operator (spectral) norm:

$$
\lVert A - A_k \rVert_F^2 \;=\; \sigma_{k+1}^2 + \sigma_{k+2}^2 + \cdots + \sigma_r^2
$$

Interpretation: the "compression error" of a rank-k approximation is exactly the sum of squares of the **discarded** singular values. Small trailing singular values → good compression. Fat trailing singular values → you're throwing away real signal, and no rank-k approximation can do better.

This is *the* theoretical backbone of every low-rank technique. The one theorem to know: **the best rank-k approximation is the top-k terms of the SVD.**

## The traps that matter

**Singular values are always non-negative and sorted descending.** They're square roots of eigenvalues of AᵀA (which is PSD, so eigenvalues ≥ 0) — no sign ambiguity. NumPy returns them in descending order; other libraries may not — always check the convention when porting code.

**Left/right singular vectors have sign ambiguity.** If (u, v, σ) is a triple, so is (−u, −v, σ). Both sign the same product `σ uv^T`, so this is truly a free choice — algorithms may return either. Don't rely on the sign of a specific singular vector across library versions.

**Rank = number of non-zero singular values.** For a "clean" theoretical matrix — sure. For an actual computed matrix with floating-point noise, "zero" means "smaller than a numerical threshold." Both `np.linalg.matrix_rank(A)` and truncation strategies choose a threshold (usually `s.max() * max(A.shape) * eps`).

**"Best rank-k" is only best in the average-error sense.** Eckart–Young says truncated SVD minimizes the *Frobenius* (sum-of-squares) error — it treats every entry equally. If your problem cares about a specific matrix structure (sparsity, non-negativity, block structure), other decompositions (NMF, sparse PCA, block-truncated SVD) can beat plain SVD on that structure. But for "unweighted mean-squared reconstruction," it's optimal.

**SVD is O(mn·min(m, n)) time.** For an m×n matrix, full SVD costs roughly min(m, n) squared times max(m, n) — same order as matrix multiplication. Truncated SVD via iterative methods (`scipy.sparse.linalg.svds`, randomized SVD) costs O(k · nnz(A)) per iteration — for the "top k only" case, use these on large matrices.

<RealWorldML title="LoRA — the SVD application every LLM engineer uses">

LoRA is a parameter-efficient fine-tuning method that has become the default way to adapt an off-the-shelf LLM to a new task. Instead of retraining a d×d weight matrix W (millions or billions of parameters per layer), LoRA *freezes* W and learns a low-rank *update* ΔW = BA parameterized by two thin matrices:

- **A** is r × d (a wide matrix)
- **B** is d × r (a tall matrix)
- **r ≪ d** (typically r ∈ \{4, 8, 16, 32\})

The forward pass becomes `y = (W + BA) x = W x + B(A x)` — the "adapter" adds a low-rank correction on top of the frozen base weight. Parameters trained: only A and B, totaling 2·d·r ≪ d². A 4096×4096 attention weight (16.8M params) becomes 2·4096·8 = 65K params with r=8 — a 260× reduction.

<RunnableCode title="LoRA storage math + SVD reconstruction of a rank-r update" code={loraCode} packages={["numpy"]} />

Read the code:

1. **The setup.** We synthesize a d×d update ΔW that is *exactly* rank r_true (constructed as U V with U, V thin), then pretend we don't know that and try to fit it with rank r_lora ≥ r_true.
2. **The reconstruction.** `U[:, :k] * s[:k] @ Vt[:k]` is the top-k truncated SVD in one expression — this is *literally* the "rank r_lora approximation" LoRA aspires to represent. When r_lora ≥ r_true (real rank of the update), the error is machine-precision zero — Eckart–Young says the low-rank approximation is exact when the target is already low-rank.
3. **Storage.** Print statement shows the parameter savings — real LLMs sit at 200× to 1000× parameter reduction from LoRA depending on `r`.

Why LoRA works empirically (a claim, not a proof): the *useful* fine-tuning update lies in a low-dimensional subspace of the full weight space. Most of the model's knowledge is in the pretrained W; adapting to a new task only requires nudging a few directions. Whether that assumption is deeply true or a lucky match to task complexity is an active research question — but the fact that LoRA works with r as small as 4 for many tasks is strong empirical evidence.

**Inference is free.** After training, you can merge `W_merged = W + B A` once and use W_merged as a normal weight — no runtime overhead. Or keep A and B separate (hot-swappable adapters) — one base model, many task-specific adapters.

Every LoRA-related paper and library boils down to this: SVD says the best rank-r approximation exists and is unique (up to sign); LoRA gives us a *learned* approximation to a specific unknown ΔW by gradient descent on A and B. The theoretical scaffolding is the chapter you're reading.

1. **Decompose and reconstruct.** Generate a random 5×3 matrix A. Compute `U, s, Vt = np.linalg.svd(A, full_matrices=False)` and verify `np.allclose(A, U @ np.diag(s) @ Vt)`. 3. **PCA via SVD.** Take a random 200×3 dataset X (centered). Compute PCA both ways: (a) eigendecomposition of `X.T @ X / (n − 1)`; (b) `U, s, Vt = np.linalg.svd(X, full_matrices=False)`. The principal components are `Vt` (from SVD) or the eigenvectors of `X.T @ X`. Verify they agree (up to sign per column). Verify that `s**2 / (n − 1)` matches the eigenvalues of the covariance — the "explained variance" values. *Hint: SVD is numerically preferable because it avoids the squaring step that creates `X.T @ X`.*

<Summary>

- **Every m×n matrix has an SVD**: A = U Σ Vᵀ with U m×m orthogonal, Σ m×n non-negative diagonal, V n×n orthogonal. No matrix-shape or non-diagonalizability caveats.
- **Geometric picture**: Vᵀ rotates the input, Σ stretches along the new axes by the singular values σ_i, U rotates into the output space. Unit sphere → rotated ellipsoid with semi-axes σ_i.
- **Relation to eigen**: right singular vectors = eigenvectors of AᵀA; singular values = square roots of those eigenvalues.
- **Full vs thin vs truncated**: full for complete orthogonal bases, thin (`full_matrices=False`) for computation, truncated (top-k) for compression.
- **Outer-product form** A = Σ σᵢ uᵢ vᵢᵀ — a sum of rank-1 pieces weighted by importance.
- **Eckart–Young**: truncated SVD is the best rank-k approximation in Frobenius (and spectral) norm; reconstruction error = Σ<sub>i&gt;k</sub> σᵢ².
- **Applications**: LoRA (low-rank fine-tuning updates), PCA (SVD of centered data avoids covariance construction), embedding compression, pseudoinverse for singular systems, attention/weight analysis.

</Summary>
