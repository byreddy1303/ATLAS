export const inverseSolveCode = `import numpy as np

# Solve Ax = b — the WRONG way and the RIGHT way.
np.random.seed(42)
A = np.random.randn(1000, 1000)
b = np.random.randn(1000)

# --- Wrong: form the explicit inverse then multiply ---
import time
t0 = time.time()
Ainv = np.linalg.inv(A)
x_wrong = Ainv @ b
t_wrong = time.time() - t0
res_wrong = np.linalg.norm(A @ x_wrong - b)

# --- Right: solve directly with a factored solver ---
t0 = time.time()

t_right = time.time() - t0
res_right = np.linalg.norm(A @ x_right - b)

print(f"inv-then-multiply : time = {t_wrong:.4f}s   residual = {res_wrong:.2e}")
print(f"solve directly    : time = {t_right:.4f}s   residual = {res_right:.2e}")
print()
print("solve is faster AND more numerically stable — never form the explicit inverse.")
`;

<Motivation title="Two questions the determinant answers in one number">

Given a linear transformation, two questions come up constantly. First: **how much does it scale volume?** The determinant of a 2×2 matrix is the (signed) area of the parallelogram the columns span — the amount by which the transformation stretches or squishes any region. Same story in 3D with volume, and in n-D with n-dimensional "volume." A determinant of 2 means "doubles all areas." A determinant of 0 means "flattens every input into a lower-dimensional subspace" — no area left.

Second: **is the transformation reversible?** A transformation that flattens things (det = 0) is fundamentally not invertible: it merges distinct inputs into a single output, and you can't undo a merge. A transformation that preserves nonzero volume (det ≠ 0) is invertible, and its inverse is a matrix A⁻¹ such that A·A⁻¹ = I. In ML terms: the input can be recovered from the output.

For a GenAI engineer, the determinant itself is almost never computed in production — but the ideas it captures (singular vs invertible, volume scaling, orientation) show up all the time: change-of-variables in normalizing flows, log-determinant in Jacobians, "well-conditioned" vs "singular" data in regression, invertibility of covariance matrices in Gaussian models. This chapter builds the geometric feel, then teaches you why `np.linalg.inv` is almost always the wrong tool — and what to use instead.

</Motivation>

<Intuition title="Determinant = signed volume scaling; inverse = do it backwards">

Picture a 2×2 matrix acting on the unit square (corners at the origin, (1,0), (1,1), (0,1)). After the transformation, the four corners land somewhere in the plane, tracing out a parallelogram. Its area is `|det A|`. If the columns of A ended up on the same line, the "parallelogram" collapsed to a line segment and the area is zero. If they flipped orientation (rotate clockwise, then reflect), the signed area is negative.

The inverse A⁻¹ is the matrix that undoes A. If A takes (1,0) to some column c₁ and (0,1) to c₂, then A⁻¹ takes c₁ back to (1,0) and c₂ back to (0,1). It exists precisely when the columns c₁, c₂ are linearly independent — that is, when the parallelogram has nonzero area — that is, when det A ≠ 0.

**Three equivalent statements** for a square matrix:

1. A is invertible.
2. det(A) ≠ 0.
3. The columns (equivalently rows) of A are linearly independent (rank = n, nullity = 0).

Any one of these implies the other two. You'll bounce between them constantly.

</Intuition>

<Recall term="Linear independence">

A set of vectors is linearly independent if no vector in the set can be written as a linear combination of the others — equivalently, if the only combination that equals the zero vector is the trivial one (all coefficients zero). For an n×n matrix, the columns being independent is equivalent to the matrix being invertible.

</Recall>

## The determinant as signed volume — geometry first

For a 2×2 matrix

$$
A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \det(A) = ad - bc.
$$

The formula is important, but the *meaning* is more important: **det(A) is the signed area of the parallelogram whose sides are the columns of A**. Sign = orientation (positive for right-handed, negative for reflected). Magnitude = area (scale factor for any region).

In n dimensions the story is identical: det(A) is the signed n-volume of the parallelepiped spanned by A's columns. And for any set S in the input space with n-volume V, the transformed set A·S has n-volume `|det(A)| · V`. This is the change-of-variables constant that shows up in every multivariable integral and every normalizing flow.

## Sign, singularity, and orientation

Three cases that classify what a 2×2 (or n×n) transformation does to volume:

- **det(A) &gt; 0**: preserves orientation and scales volume by det(A). Pure rotations have det = 1. A shear has det = 1. A scaling by k in every axis has det = kⁿ (for n×n).
- **det(A) &lt; 0**: reverses orientation (mirrors) and scales volume by `|det(A)|`. A single reflection in 2D has det = −1.
- **det(A) = 0**: flattens the input into a lower-dimensional subspace. Rank &lt; n. No inverse. Also called *singular*.

The equivalence det = 0 ⇔ singular ⇔ non-invertible ⇔ rank &lt; n is one of the sharpest invariants in linear algebra. Any one of these fails? All of them fail.

## Key properties of the determinant (worth memorizing)

For any n×n matrices A, B and scalar c:

1. **det(AB) = det(A) · det(B)** — determinant is multiplicative. Combining transformations multiplies their volume-scaling factors. Beautiful and useful.
2. **det(Aᵀ) = det(A)** — a transformation and its transpose have the same volume scaling.
3. **det(A⁻¹) = 1 / det(A)** (when A is invertible). Undoing a scaling multiplies volume by the reciprocal.
4. **det(cA) = cⁿ · det(A)** for n×n A. Scaling every entry by c scales volume by cⁿ, because you're scaling each of n dimensions independently.
5. **Triangular matrix** (upper or lower): determinant = product of diagonal entries. This is why LU factorization lets you compute determinants in O(n³) instead of O(n!): factor A = LU, then det(A) = product of U's diagonal (since det(L) = 1 when unit-diagonal).

You'll use (1) and (5) constantly. Change-of-variables in a chain of transformations? Product of individual determinants. Compute a big determinant? Factor into triangular pieces.

## The inverse — what it is, and when to compute it

For an n×n matrix A with det(A) ≠ 0, the **inverse** A⁻¹ is the unique matrix satisfying:

$$
A \cdot A^{-1} \;=\; A^{-1} \cdot A \;=\; I_n
$$

Geometrically: A⁻¹ undoes A. If A rotates by 30° and scales by 2, then A⁻¹ scales by 1/2 and rotates by −30°.

The 2×2 formula (memorize this one — comes up all the time in derivations):

$$
A^{-1} \;=\; \frac{1}{\det(A)} \begin{bmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{bmatrix} \quad \text{where } A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}.
$$

For n×n with n ≥ 3, don't use the general formula (Cramer's rule via adjugate). Real code computes the inverse via LU or QR factorization in O(n³) time — but usually you never need the inverse *matrix*, you just need to solve A·x = b for some b. Which brings us to the pitfall.

## The pitfall — never form the explicit inverse

You need `x = A⁻¹·b`. It is very tempting to write:

```python
x = np.linalg.inv(A) @ b        # BAD
```

Almost always, you should write:

```python
x = np.linalg.solve(A, b)       # GOOD
```

Why `solve` is better in every real setting:

- **Faster.** Forming A⁻¹ is one full LU factorization plus n extra back-substitutions (to invert). `solve(A, b)` is one LU factorization plus one back-substitution. Roughly 2–3× less work.
- **More accurate.** Forming A⁻¹ then multiplying accumulates two rounds of floating-point error. `solve` does one round. Difference is small for well-conditioned A, disastrous for ill-conditioned A.
- **Uses less memory.** A⁻¹ is a full n×n matrix; `solve` reuses the factored form and only produces the n-vector answer.

The rule: **treat A⁻¹ as a mathematical abstraction, not a computational plan.** Whenever you see A⁻¹ in a formula, translate it to "solve the linear system" in code. This is the single biggest gap between textbook linear algebra and real numerical practice.

## Condition number — the "how sensitive is the inverse" gauge

Even when det(A) ≠ 0, the inverse can be numerically unusable if A is *close* to singular. The **condition number** κ(A) = ‖A‖ · ‖A⁻¹‖ measures how much A amplifies small errors in b when you solve A·x = b:

- κ(A) close to 1 → **well-conditioned**. Small changes in b cause proportionally small changes in x.
- κ(A) very large (say, &gt; 10¹⁰) → **ill-conditioned**. Tiny changes in b or in A itself cause huge changes in x. Floating-point rounding errors alone can wreck the answer.

In numerical practice, a "singular" matrix isn't just one with det = 0; it's one with κ so large that noise-level inputs produce garbage-level outputs. `np.linalg.cond(A)` computes it; SVD gives it as σ_max / σ_min (the ratio of the largest to smallest singular value).

## Why "explicit inverse" is even worse than it looks

Aside from being slower, the explicit inverse leaks a subtle bug: **A⁻¹ almost always has full-rank, dense structure even when A has helpful sparsity or banded structure.** A sparse matrix A (mostly zeros) has an inverse A⁻¹ that is usually dense (mostly nonzeros). Storing the inverse of a 10⁶ × 10⁶ sparse matrix is impossible; solving A·x = b via sparse LU or an iterative method (conjugate gradient, GMRES) uses vastly less memory.

Modern ML almost never stores an explicit inverse:

- Gaussian process regression solves `(K + σ²I) α = y` using Cholesky, never forms the inverse.
- Kalman filter updates use `S = H P Hᵀ + R` and then `K = P Hᵀ · S⁻¹` — but the `· S⁻¹` is done via `solve(S, ·)` not `inv(S)`.
- Second-order optimizers (Newton, natural gradient) compute Hessian-vector products via `solve(H, g)`, never forming H⁻¹ (which is dense even for banded H).

Once you internalize "matrix inverse = math notation, solve = code," you'll write faster, more accurate ML pipelines without thinking about it.

<RealWorldML title="Log-determinant in normalizing flows — the change-of-variables trick that turns density estimation into function inversion">

A normalizing flow is a family of invertible neural networks used for density estimation (and sometimes sampling). The core idea: pick a simple base distribution (say, a standard Gaussian in ℝᵈ), and learn an invertible function f that maps samples from that Gaussian to samples from your data distribution. The trained f defines a probability density on data space via the **change-of-variables formula**:

$$
p_{\mathrm{data}}(x) \;=\; p_{\mathrm{base}}\!\bigl(f^{-1}(x)\bigr) \;\cdot\; \bigl|\det J_{f^{-1}}(x)\bigr|
$$

That determinant term is the volume-scaling factor of the inverse map at each point x — exactly the geometric fact we started this chapter with. If f stretches a small region near f⁻¹(x) by a factor of 10 in volume, then the density in that region on the data side is 1/10 of the base density at f⁻¹(x) (probability mass is conserved; if the box got 10× bigger, the density is 10× smaller).

Training a flow means maximizing log p_data(x) over the training data, which by the formula is:

`log p_data(x)  =  log p_base(f⁻¹(x))  +  log |det J(x)|`

The `log |det J(x)|` term is the *log-determinant of the Jacobian* — one number per data point, from an n×n matrix. Computed naively that's O(n³) per data point, which is prohibitive when n is a few thousand (say, an image flattened to a vector). This is *why* normalizing flow architectures (RealNVP, Glow, IAF) are all built around clever choices of f whose Jacobian is triangular — so `det = product of diagonal entries`, computable in O(n).

Every design choice in a flow architecture — coupling layers, autoregressive parameterizations, affine transformations with easily-invertible parts — comes back to the twin needs of the change-of-variables formula: (1) f must be invertible, and (2) `log |det J|` must be cheap to compute. Two requirements, both direct consequences of the geometric meaning of the determinant.

You'll see the same structure in variational autoencoders (the reparameterization trick uses a change-of-variables), in the diffusion-model derivation of the ELBO, and in any generative model that reasons about densities under a learned map. Volume scaling + invertibility = the whole story.

</RealWorldML>

<Summary>

- The **determinant** of an n×n matrix A is the signed n-volume of the parallelepiped spanned by its columns. Positive = orientation preserved; negative = orientation flipped; zero = flattened (singular).
- For any n×n matrix, **A invertible ⇔ det(A) ≠ 0 ⇔ rank(A) = n ⇔ nullspace = zero-vector only.** Four ways to say the same thing.
- **Multiplicativity: det(AB) = det(A) · det(B).** Also det(cA) = cⁿ · det(A), det(Aᵀ) = det(A), det(A⁻¹) = 1/det(A). Triangular ⇒ det = product of diagonal.
- **The inverse** A⁻¹ is the unique matrix with A·A⁻¹ = I. 2×2 formula: swap diagonals, negate off-diagonals, divide by det. For n ≥ 3, don't use closed-form.
- **In code, never form the explicit inverse.** Use `np.linalg.solve(A, b)` instead of `np.linalg.inv(A) @ b` — faster, more accurate, less memory. Rule: A⁻¹ is math notation, solve() is the code.
- **Condition number κ(A)** = ratio of largest to smallest singular value = how much A amplifies input errors. Small det doesn't mean unstable; huge κ does.
- ML uses log-determinants of Jacobians in normalizing flows (change of variables), pushes Cholesky/LU/QR instead of explicit inverses everywhere (Gaussian processes, Kalman filters, Newton methods), and treats the invertibility trinity as a first-class check on regression, covariance estimation, and any linear inverse problem.

</Summary>
