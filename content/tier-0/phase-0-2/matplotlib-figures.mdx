<Motivation title="The chart is the interface between your numbers and every decision made about them">

You now know how to compute almost anything about a table: means per group, joins across sources, ten-million-row reductions in milliseconds. Here's the uncomfortable next fact: **nobody — including you — can read the raw output.** Print a 30-row groupby result and your eyes glaze at row six. Print `describe()` for twelve columns and the one broken column hides in the noise. Human working memory holds about four numbers; your datasets hold millions. The only high-bandwidth channel into a human brain is the *visual* one — and a chart is how you put data on it.

This isn't a soft skill bolted onto the real work. In 1973, statistician Francis Anscombe constructed four small datasets — now famous as *Anscombe's quartet* — with **identical** means, variances, correlations, and regression lines. Same statistics, to two decimal places. Plotted, they are obviously four completely different worlds: a clean linear trend, a curve, a line with one outlier dragging it, and a vertical stack with one point flung far right. Every summary statistic you learned to compute would call them the same. One scatter plot each exposes the truth in half a second. **Statistics summarize; plots reveal.** You will meet real Anscombes constantly — the "flat" metric that's actually two populations moving oppositely, the "strong correlation" that's one outlier, the sensor that flatlined for a week.

And in ML specifically, one chart family runs the whole show: **training curves**. Train loss and validation loss per epoch — that picture is how you diagnose overfitting (the gap opens), underfitting (both stuck high), a broken learning rate (loss explodes or zigzags), or data leakage (validation suspiciously *better* than train). Every serious ML engineer stares at these curves daily; tools like TensorBoard and Weights & Biases are, at heart, machines for drawing them. You'll build one from scratch in this chapter.

So why matplotlib, specifically? Because it is the substrate. pandas' `.plot()`, seaborn, TensorBoard's charts, scikit-learn's display utilities, half the figures in every arXiv paper — all matplotlib underneath, all returning matplotlib `Axes` objects you customize with the API you learn here. Learn the two-object model once and every plotting tool in the Python universe becomes "matplotlib with a head start."

One warning before we start: matplotlib has *two* APIs — an implicit state-machine style (`plt.plot(...)`) that tutorials love because it saves a line, and an explicit object style (`ax.plot(...)`) that production code requires. The implicit style is this chapter's designated villain: it works right up until you write a function, and then it fails in the most confusing way possible. We'll desugar exactly why, and you'll never be confused by someone else's `plt.` code again.

</Motivation>

<Intuition title="A Figure is a sheet of paper; an Axes is one chart drawn on it">

Matplotlib's entire object model is two nouns:

The **Figure** is the sheet of paper — the canvas, the file you'll save, the window that pops up. It has a size in inches (`figsize=(7, 4)`), a resolution (`dpi`), and it *contains* things. On its own it displays nothing but background.

The **Axes** is one complete chart living on that paper: the x-axis and y-axis (each an `Axis` object — mind the spelling: *Axes* is the chart, *Axis* is one ruler), the plotted lines and bars, the title, the labels, the legend, the grid, the four border lines (called **spines**). When you think "the chart," you mean the Axes. One Figure can hold one Axes (the common case) or a grid of them (**small multiples** — six little charts sharing one sheet).

Everything you draw is an **artist** — a Line2D, a Rectangle (each bar of a bar chart), a Text (the title). Artists live on an Axes; Axes live on a Figure. That's the whole containment hierarchy: **Figure → Axes → artists.** Every matplotlib call you'll ever make is "create an artist on this Axes" or "adjust a property of something that exists." Nothing else is going on.

The one line that hands you both objects, and the first line of essentially every real matplotlib script on Earth:

```python
fig, ax = plt.subplots()
```

From there, the division of labor is clean. Talk to `ax` for anything *inside* the chart: `ax.plot(...)`, `ax.set_title(...)`, `ax.set_xlabel(...)`, `ax.legend()`. Talk to `fig` for anything about the *sheet*: `fig.savefig(...)`, `fig.tight_layout()`, overall size. When you want six charts: `fig, axs = plt.subplots(2, 3)` — one sheet, six Axes in a 2×3 grid, and you draw on `axs[0, 0]`, `axs[0, 1]`, ... explicitly.

Hold that mental picture — paper, charts on the paper, artists on the charts — and the API stops being 3,000 functions and becomes one sentence: *get the objects, then set properties on them.*

</Intuition>

<Recall term="ndarray">

From the NumPy chapter: the **ndarray** is a typed slab of numbers with a shape. Matplotlib is an ndarray consumer — `ax.plot(x, y)` wants two same-length arrays, `ax.hist(values)` wants one, `ax.scatter` wants two (plus optional arrays for size and color). All the data-shaping happens *before* the plot call, with the vectorized tools you already have: `np.linspace` for smooth curves, comparisons for masks, reductions for the numbers you annotate. Matplotlib draws; NumPy decides *what* gets drawn.

</Recall>

<Recall term="DataFrame → arrays">

From the pandas chapters: a DataFrame is labeled columns, and `.to_numpy()` (or just passing a Series) drops the labels when a library wants bare arrays. Plotting is friendlier than scikit-learn here — you can hand `ax.plot` a Series directly (it uses the values), or skip the manual handoff entirely with `df.plot(ax=ax)`, which reads the *index* as x, columns as series, and column *names* as legend labels. The labels you maintained all chapter finally pay rent as automatic chart annotations.

</Recall>

<Recall term="NaN">

The float that poisons reductions has one *useful* superpower in matplotlib: **a NaN breaks the line.** `ax.plot` skips NaN points, leaving a visible gap instead of connecting across missing data — which is honest (the data *was* missing) and diagnostic (gaps show you exactly where). A sensor that died for two days should show a hole, not a confident straight bridge. When you *want* the bridge, you interpolate explicitly first — a deliberate modeling decision, exactly like `fillna` was.

</Recall>

    Run the cell and read it as a tour of the containment hierarchy. `plt.subplots(figsize=(7.2, 3.6))` manufactures the *sheet* (Figure, 7.2×3.6 inches) with one *chart* (Axes) on it and hands you both. Every subsequent line is "set a property on an object":

    - `ax.plot(x, np.sin(x), ...)` creates a **Line2D artist** on the Axes and returns it. Call `plot` again → a second artist on the same Axes. The final `print` proves it: `ax.lines` is a real list holding both.

    - `ax.legend()` reads the `label=` you attached to each line and builds the key. No labels, no legend — which is why the habit is to label *at plot time*.
    - The **spines** — `ax.spines["top"]` etc. — are the four border lines. Hiding top and right is the standard modern look (less ink competing with data); we also recolor the survivors because this site is dark. On your machine you'd skip the colors and keep the two-spine hiding.
    - `ax.tick_params` styles the little rulers; `ax.grid` adds reference lines *behind* the data.

    Notice what you never touched: any global state. Every mark on that chart traces to an explicit method call on an object you can see in the code. That property — **the code is the complete recipe** — is what makes the object style debuggable, reviewable, and function-safe. Six months from now, "why is there a legend?" has a greppable answer.

    The counter-model to keep in your head: this is *not* like `print`, where output just goes "to the screen." Charts are built like LEGO — objects added to objects — and displayed only at the end (here, the site captures the finished Figure; on your laptop, `plt.show()` or `fig.savefig` does it). Between construction and display, everything is still editable — that's what makes the ecosystem angle (pandas drawing on *your* ax) possible.

  <Angle type="pitfall" title="The pyplot state machine — why plt.plot works in tutorials and betrays you in functions">
    <RunnableCode title="two chart functions, one current axes — watch them collide" code={stateCode} captureFig />

    Every matplotlib tutorial ever written starts `plt.plot([1, 2, 3])` — no Figure, no Axes, and it *works*. How? Because `pyplot` (the `plt` module) is a **state machine**: it silently tracks a "current figure" and "current axes," and every `plt.something()` call means "do that to whatever is current." No current axes exists? `plt.plot` *creates* one for you. That implicit magic is wonderful for a three-line notebook experiment and catastrophic the day you organize code into functions.

    The cell stages the accident honestly. `plot_revenue()` and `plot_signups()` are the kind of helpers everyone writes first — each intends to make "its own" chart. But neither creates a figure, so the first call manufactures one, and the second call happily draws onto *the same current axes*: two unrelated series mashed together, the second title overwriting the first. The prints measure the wreckage — one figure alive, two lines on it. In real projects the same bug wears worse costumes: a chart function called in a loop stacks twenty series onto one axes; a library function you imported quietly draws onto *your* current figure; two notebook cells run out of order swap whose chart gets which title.

    The fix is a one-sentence policy, and it's the fix the whole Python ecosystem converged on: **functions never touch `plt.*` for drawing — they accept an `ax` parameter and draw on it.**

    ```python
    def plot_revenue(ax):
        ax.plot(days, revenue, label="revenue")
        ax.set_title("revenue")

    fig, (a1, a2) = plt.subplots(1, 2)
    plot_revenue(a1)
    plot_signups(a2)
    ```

    The *caller* owns layout — one chart or a 2×3 grid, the helper neither knows nor cares. This is the same lesson the pandas chapter taught with chained indexing: implicit intermediate state (there, an unnamed copy; here, a hidden "current axes") is where bugs breed; explicit objects are where they die. Reserve `plt` for exactly three jobs — `plt.subplots()` to create, `plt.show()` to display, `plt.close()` to dispose — and let every mark on every chart be a method on a named `ax`.

  </Angle>

  <Angle type="workflow" title="The five-line skeleton — create, draw, label, legend, annotate">
    <RunnableCode title="the ritual, on a loss curve" code={skeletonCode} captureFig />

    Muscle memory time. Every finished chart you make for the next several years is this skeleton with different data:

    1. **Create** — `fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(w, h))`. Size in *inches*; pick it deliberately because it controls how big fonts look relative to data (a chart destined for a slide wants a smaller figsize / bigger effective text than one for a report).
    2. **Draw** — `ax.plot(x, y, ...)` with style decided *at the call*: `color`, `lw` (line width), `marker`, and crucially `label=` for the legend. Second series? Second `ax.plot`. Different question? Different verb — `bar`, `hist`, `scatter` — same skeleton.
    3. **Label** — `ax.set_xlabel`, `ax.set_ylabel`, `dress(ax, title)`. Non-negotiable: an unlabeled axis makes the chart unreadable to anyone but present-you, and present-you expires in about a week. Units belong in labels ("loss", "epoch", "revenue ($k)").
    4. **Legend** — `ax.legend()` whenever two or more series share an Axes. `frameon=False` is the clean look.
    5. **Annotate** — the professional finishing move. `ax.annotate(text, xy=point, xytext=where_the_text_sits, arrowprops=...)` plants a labeled arrow on *the one point that matters* — the minimum, the anomaly, the launch date. A chart states facts; an annotation states the *finding*. Readers' eyes go where you point.

    Then the exit: `fig.tight_layout()` (fixes clipped labels — just always call it), and either `fig.savefig("name.png", dpi=200, bbox_inches="tight")` for a file or `plt.show()` for a window. On this site, the Pyodide cell captures the figure for you — which is also why you don't see `plt.show()` in these cells.

    The helper `dress(ax, ...)` in the cell is worth stealing as a *pattern*: any styling you type three times becomes a function taking `ax` — exactly the ax-passing discipline from the pitfall angle, now working *for* you. (The ambient reason: this site is dark, so cells restyle spines and ticks; on your white-background machine the defaults are fine and `dress` would just be your house style.)

  </Angle>

  <Angle type="taste" title="Choose the chart from the question, not from the gallery">
    <RunnableCode title="four question types → four charts, as small multiples" code={selectCode} captureFig />

    Walk into the matplotlib gallery and you'll drown — hundreds of chart types, most of which you will never need. Taste here is a four-row lookup table. Ask what *question* the chart answers:

    - **"How are values distributed?"** → **histogram** (`ax.hist`). One numeric column → its shape: where values pile up, how far the tail stretches, whether there are two humps (two populations hiding in one column — an Anscombe in the wild). The `bins=` knob matters: too few hides structure, too many turns to noise; try 20–50 and look.
    - **"How do categories compare?"** → **bar** (`ax.bar`). A few named things, one number each — churn per plan, accuracy per model. Bars encode *length*, which is why the honesty angle will insist they start at zero.
    - **"Are two quantities related?"** → **scatter** (`ax.scatter`). Two numeric columns, one dot per row. Correlation, clusters, outliers — all visible instantly, none reliably summarizable (Anscombe again). With thousands of points, drop `alpha` to ~0.3 and size `s` down so density reads as darkness.
    - **"How does it change over time (or order)?"** → **line** (`ax.plot`). The x-axis has an inherent ordering — timestamps, epochs, weeks. Lines imply *continuity between points*, which is exactly the claim you're making about a trend, and exactly why a line through unordered categories is wrong.

    That's the whole starter kit: four verbs cover ~90% of working data science. (The next tier of usefulness — `boxplot` for distribution-per-category, `imshow` for matrices like confusion matrices and attention maps — arrives when EDA and ML chapters need them.)

    The cell also smuggles in the layout tool you'll use constantly: **small multiples** — `plt.subplots(2, 2)` returning an *array* of Axes, one small honest chart per question, sharing one sheet. Four modest panels beat one heroic chart-of-everything, because each panel makes exactly one comparison. Note the indexing (`axs[0, 0]` — it's a 2-D ndarray of Axes objects; your NumPy indexing works on charts now) and the loop-friendliness: `for ax, col in zip(axs.flat, columns): ...` is the idiom for plotting a dozen features fast.

  </Angle>

  <Angle type="production" title="Honest scales — the axis is part of the claim">
    <RunnableCode title="one dataset, two stories: the truncated-axis lie, side by side with the truth" code={honestCode} captureFig />

    The same two numbers — 96.1 and 97.3 — told two ways. On the left, the y-axis starts at 95.8 and "ours" towers over the baseline at roughly four times its height: a chart that *screams breakthrough*. On the right, the axis starts at zero and you see the truth — a real but modest 1.2-point improvement. Same data. The **axis limits are part of the claim**, and the left panel's claim is false.

    The rule and its boundary, precisely: **bar charts must start at zero, always** — bars encode value as *length*, and truncating the axis clips every bar by the same amount, which distorts their *ratio* (the very thing bars exist to show). **Line charts may zoom** — lines encode value as *position*, and zooming into ±0.5° of temperature variation is often the only way to see the signal at all. The test: if the chart invites "A is twice B" comparisons (bars do), the baseline must be zero; if it shows *shape of change* (lines), zoom deliberately and label the axis so the zoom is visible. When someone hands you a truncated bar chart — cable news, a vendor deck, a paper's figure 3 — you now know exactly which line of code committed the crime: `set_ylim`.

    Three more production honesty habits, cheap to adopt:

    - **Shared scales for compared panels.** Small multiples comparing the same quantity must share axis limits (`sharey=True`), or panel-to-panel comparison silently lies the same way truncation does. (The training-curves cell below uses `sharex` for aligned epochs.)
    - **Annotate the takeaway, cite the units.** The right panel's `+1.2 pts` and the dashed baseline do the reader's arithmetic for them — the difference between a chart someone *glances at* and one they *repeat in a meeting*.
    - **Color is meaning, so spend it.** One accent color for the thing you're arguing about, gray for context (exactly what the cell does — lime for "ours," ink-gray for baseline). Rainbow-everything charts spend the reader's attention on decoding instead of deciding. And never encode meaning in color *alone* — about 1 in 12 men can't separate red from green; pair color with position, label, or line style.

    None of this is aesthetics for its own sake. A chart is an argument; these rules are what make it a *sound* one — able to survive the code review, the skeptical PM, and reviewer #2.

  </Angle>

  <Angle type="ecosystem" title="Everything returns an Axes — pandas, seaborn, scikit-learn all draw on your canvas">
    <RunnableCode title="pandas draws the data, matplotlib finishes the chart" code={ecosystemCode} captureFig />

    Here's the payoff of learning the object model rather than memorized incantations: **the entire Python plotting ecosystem speaks Axes.** The cell shows the pattern with pandas — `df.plot(ax=ax)` — and it generalizes everywhere.

    pandas' `.plot()` accessor is a convenience wrapper: it reads the DataFrame's *index* as x, plots each *column* as a series, uses column *names* as legend labels, and — the crucial part — **draws onto the matplotlib Axes you pass via `ax=`**. It saves you the `for col in df: ax.plot(...)` loop and the manual date handling. But it stops at "data on screen." Titles, honest limits, annotations, spine styling, the finishing that makes a chart communicate? That's you, talking to the same `ax` afterwards with the exact API from this chapter. Division of labor: *high-level library draws the data, raw matplotlib finishes the chart.* One new matplotlib-ism sneaks in: `fig.autofmt_xdate()` tilts date labels so they don't overlap — the standard move for time axes.

    The same contract runs through the whole stack you'll meet later: **seaborn** (statistical charts — most functions take and return `ax=`), **scikit-learn's displays** (`ConfusionMatrixDisplay`, ROC curves — all expose the underlying `ax_`), **statsmodels** diagnostics, even pandas' `hist()` and `boxplot()`. The workflow is always: let the specialist library produce the 80% chart in one line, then customize the remaining 20% with `ax.set_*` because you know what an Axes *is*.

    This is also the argument for *not* learning "a simpler plotting library instead." Tutorials pitch seaborn or plotly-express as escapes from matplotlib's verbosity — but the moment you need to change *anything* the wrapper didn't anticipate (and you always do: the title, a reference line, that one annotation), you're back at the Axes API. Learn the substrate once — you just did — and every wrapper becomes a time-saver instead of a wall.

  </Angle>

<Convergence>

Every lens lands on the same object model. **A Figure is the sheet; Axes are the charts on it; everything drawn is an artist owned by an Axes** — and all real code holds these objects explicitly (`fig, ax = plt.subplots()`) instead of trusting pyplot's hidden "current axes," which is exactly the implicit-state trap the pandas chapter taught you to distrust. On that foundation, craft is a short checklist: pick the chart from the *question* (distribution/comparison/relationship/trend), run the five-line skeleton (create → draw → label → legend → annotate), keep the axes honest (bars from zero, shared scales across compared panels), and let higher-level libraries draw onto *your* `ax` before you finish the chart by hand.

The deeper convergence: a chart is not decoration on the analysis — it *is* the analysis, delivered. Anscombe's quartet showed identical statistics hiding four different truths; the training curves you're about to build show a model's whole biography in two panels. Numbers compute; pictures communicate; you now own the machine that turns one into the other.

<Derivation title="What actually happens when you call plt.plot(...) — the state machine, desugared">

Not math — the hidden machinery behind the two-API confusion, one step at a time. After this, no `plt.` code will ever mystify you again.

<Step n={1} math={<code>plt.plot(xs, ys)</code>}>
  The tutorial one-liner. No Figure exists, no Axes exists, yet a chart appears. Something is doing object creation behind your back — the question is what, and where it hides its state.
</Step>

<Step n={2} math={<code>ax = plt.gca()</code>}>
  Inside, <code>plt.plot</code> is nearly one line: <em>get the current axes, forward everything to it</em>. <code>gca()</code> — &quot;get current axes&quot; — asks the hidden state machine for whichever Axes is &quot;current.&quot; If none exists, <strong>it creates one</strong> (on the current figure) and blesses it as current. That silent auto-creation is the entire magic of step 1.
</Step>

<Step n={3} math={<code>fig = plt.gcf()  ·  Gcf.figs = {"{"}1: fig{"}"}</code>}>
  One level deeper: <code>gca()</code> itself calls <code>gcf()</code> — &quot;get current figure&quot; — which consults a module-level registry (a class literally named <code>Gcf</code>) mapping figure numbers to live figures, again auto-creating on empty. So the full chain is: <em>module global → current figure → current axes → forward the call</em>. Every <code>plt.title</code>, <code>plt.xlabel</code>, <code>plt.legend</code> walks this same chain — they all mean &quot;...on whatever is current.&quot;
</Step>

<Step n={4} math={<span>{"two functions × one 'current' = collision"}</span>}>
  Now the pitfall-angle bug is mechanical, not mysterious. <code>plot_revenue()</code> runs: registry empty → figure 1 created → axes created → line drawn. <code>plot_signups()</code> runs: registry <em>not</em> empty → same figure, same &quot;current&quot; axes → second line and a title overwrite land on the first chart. Neither function did anything wrong <em>locally</em> — the shared mutable global did it. It&apos;s the same disease as pandas&apos; global RNG (<code>np.random.seed</code>) and the chained-assignment temporary: <strong>invisible shared state plus innocent-looking calls</strong>. And it explains the notebook illusion: in a fresh cell-by-cell notebook, &quot;current&quot; usually happens to be what you meant — until you re-run cells out of order, and suddenly it isn&apos;t.
</Step>

<Step n={5} math={<code>fig, ax = plt.subplots()  →  ax.plot(xs, ys)</code>}>
  The object API is the same machinery with the state <em>reified into variables you hold</em>. <code>subplots()</code> creates the Figure and Axes and hands them to you; <code>ax.plot</code> skips <code>gca()</code> entirely — no lookup, no auto-creation, no &quot;current.&quot; Which chart gets the line? The one whose name is on the call. That&apos;s the whole difference between the two APIs: <strong>pyplot resolves targets through hidden globals; the object API resolves them through your variable names.</strong> Same artists, same rendering — one of them is just legible.
</Step>

</Derivation>

<Nuance>

The traps and fine print, in rough order of how soon they'll bite:

- **`plt.show()` behaves differently by habitat.** Scripts: it *blocks* — code stops until you close the window; call it once, at the end. Notebooks: usually unnecessary (`%matplotlib inline` displays figures automatically after each cell). This site: the cell captures the current figure itself — that's why you don't see `show()` above. Order matters too: **`savefig` before `show`** — show can flush/clear the figure, and saving after it writes blank PNGs on some backends. The classic "my saved file is empty" bug.

- **Figures leak.** Every `plt.subplots()` registers a live figure that Python keeps until `plt.close(fig)`. Make one per training epoch in a loop and you'll meet the warning `More than 20 figures have been opened` on the way to an out-of-memory. The pattern for chart-per-item loops: create, `savefig`, **`plt.close(fig)`** — every iteration.

- **`tight_layout()` is the cure for clipped labels.** Rotated tick labels or a y-label sliced off at the edge means layout ran with defaults; `fig.tight_layout()` (or creating with `plt.subplots(constrained_layout=True)`, its smarter successor) fixes 95% of cases. Just always call it — you'll notice the 5% when a colorbar gets weird.

- **`figsize` and `dpi` fight over "how big."** `figsize` is inches; `dpi` is dots per inch; pixels = product. But *fonts* are set in points (an absolute unit) — so a small figsize blown up to full-slide width shows **huge** text, and a poster-size figsize shrunk into a column shows *microscopic* text. Rule: set figsize to the *final display size*, then `dpi=200`-ish at save time for sharpness; if text looks wrong, fix figsize, not font sizes.

- **Log scales are a tool and a trap.** `ax.set_yscale("log")` is *right* for quantities spanning decades (loss curves that fall 1000×, word frequencies, model-size scaling laws — Tier 4 will live here) — a straight line on log-y means exponential change. The trap: log axes visually compress *huge* differences into small distances, so label loudly. And log-anything with zeros or negatives in the data simply errors — filter first.

- **Dates deserve their own chapter but get three habits**: keep them as real datetimes (pandas datetime64 → matplotlib handles them natively), call `fig.autofmt_xdate()` to tilt labels, and plot *sorted-by-time* data or lines will scribble back and forth.

- **Big scatters need transparency math.** 100k points at `alpha=1` is a solid blob; `alpha=0.1, s=4` turns overplotting into a density map. Past ~1M, switch tools (`hexbin`, or histogram the data first).

- **The `axs` from `plt.subplots(2, 2)` is an ndarray**, so 1-row/1-col calls return different shapes (`subplots(1, 2)` → 1-D array; `subplots(1, 1)` → bare Axes). The loop-safe idiom: `axs = np.atleast_1d(axs).ravel()`, or just `axs.flat` when iterating.

</Nuance>

<RealWorldML title="Training curves — the two-panel chart every ML engineer reads daily">

<RunnableCode title="loss + accuracy panels with early-stopping annotation" code={realWorldCode} captureFig />

This is the chart. Not "a" chart — *the* chart: the one you will draw (or have TensorBoard/W&B draw) for every model you ever train. It answers the only question that matters mid-training — *is this run healthy, and when should it stop?* The cell fakes a realistic 30-epoch run (exponential loss decay, noisy validation, overfitting onset after epoch 14) so the diagnosis is visible, then builds the standard two-panel readout.

**Line-by-line:**

- `train_loss = 2.2 * np.exp(-epochs / 7) + 0.08 + noise` — vectorized curve-building, no loops: exponential decay (the canonical shape of a learning curve) plus a floor (models never reach zero loss) plus Gaussian jitter. `val_loss` gets a higher floor (validation is always a bit worse — the model never saw it) **plus** `np.where(epochs > 14, (epochs - 14) * 0.028, 0)`: after epoch 14, validation loss starts *climbing* while train keeps falling. That divergence is **overfitting** — the model has stopped learning generalizable patterns and started memorizing training quirks. You'll meet the theory in Tier 2; you now already own its *picture*.
- `best = int(val_loss.argmin())` — the argmax/argmin reflex from NumPy, deciding a real thing: the epoch whose weights you'd actually keep. Stopping there instead of training on is **early stopping**, and this line is its entire logic.
- `plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(8.0, 3.5), sharex=True)` — small multiples with a *shared x*: both panels agree on what "epoch 14" means, so your eye can trace one vertical line across both. That's the shared-scales honesty rule doing real work.
- `L.fill_between(epochs, train_loss, val_loss, alpha=0.12)` — shades the **generalization gap**, the vertical distance between the curves. The gap *is* the diagnosis: parallel-and-small = healthy; opening like a mouth = overfitting; both-high = underfitting. `fill_between` turns an abstract quantity into a visible wedge — annotation by geometry.
- `axvline(epochs[best], ls="--")` on *both* panels plus `annotate("best val @ ...")` — the finding, marked. A reader who sees only this figure for five seconds still leaves with the run's one actionable fact: *ship epoch 14's checkpoint.*
- The right panel plots `val_acc` with `set_ylim(0.5, 1.0)` — a *line* chart legitimately zoomed (position encoding, shape-of-change question — the taste angle's boundary case, used correctly), showing the same story in the metric your stakeholders actually ask about: accuracy plateaus exactly where loss bottoms out, then decays.
- The commented `fig.savefig("training_curves.png", dpi=200, bbox_inches="tight")` is the line that, in a real training script, runs every N epochs so you can watch the run from your phone. Chart-in-a-loop — which is why the Nuance section's `plt.close(fig)` habit exists.

When you meet TensorBoard and Weights & Biases in the deep-learning tier, you'll recognize them instantly: they are *this figure*, automated, live-updating, with a slider. The skill of *reading* it — gap means overfit, floor means capacity, zigzag means learning-rate chaos — transfers unchanged, because you built it from raw arrays once, right here.

<LifeLesson title="You live by the charts you draw of yourself — check the axes">

Everyone runs a private dashboard — mental charts of "how I'm doing": career trajectory, fitness, savings, learning. The lesson of the honest-scales angle is that **the frame does as much work as the data**, and most people frame themselves dishonestly without noticing.

The truncated axis is the anxiety machine. Compare this month's progress to last month's on a zoomed-in scale and every wobble looks like a crisis — a 1% dip renders as a cliff, exactly like the 95.8-baseline bar chart. Zoom out to the zero baseline — where you actually started, three years ago — and the same wobble vanishes into an unmistakable upward line. Both charts are "true." One of them is honest about magnitudes. When a bad week feels like collapse, you're usually reading a truncated axis; re-plot from zero before you conclude anything.

The unshared scale is the comparison trap. Measuring your chapter 3 against someone else's chapter 30 is two panels with different y-limits — the comparison *looks* valid (both are charts of "success"!) and means nothing, the same silent lie as small multiples without `sharey`. Comparisons only carry information when the axes match: same starting point, same units, same time window. Mostly, the only panel that shares your scale is your own past self.

And the deepest one comes from Anscombe: **summary statistics hide what pictures reveal.** A single number — GPA, salary, follower count, weight — can stay flat while the underlying shape of your life changes completely, for better or worse. Don't govern yourself by one aggregate. Plot the distribution: where the hours actually go, which days were good, what the outliers were. Then annotate the finding — name the one thing the picture shows — because a chart without an annotation is data without a decision, on dashboards and in Decembers alike.

<Summary>

- **Two objects:** Figure = the sheet (size, saving); Axes = one chart on it (data, labels, legend, spines). Everything drawn is an artist owned by an Axes. `fig, ax = plt.subplots()` is line one, forever.
